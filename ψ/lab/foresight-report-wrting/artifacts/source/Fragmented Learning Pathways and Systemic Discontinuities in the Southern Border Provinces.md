
# Fragmented Learning Pathways and Systemic Discontinuities in the Southern Border Provinces: An Analysis of Educational Transitions and Competency Recognition

The Southern Border Provinces (SBP) of Thailand—primarily Pattani, Yala, Narathiwat, and four districts of Songkhla—represent a unique educational landscape defined by the intersection of deep-rooted religious traditions, ethnolinguistic identity, and the persistent challenge of political unrest.1 For children and youth in this region, learning is not a linear progression through a single state system; rather, it is a navigation through a complex mosaic of mainstream public schools, private Islamic institutions, traditional pondoks, and non-formal pathways.2 These pathways often operate with differing mandates: while the state seeks to promote national unity and modernization through the Basic Education Core Curriculum, local communities prioritize the preservation of Malay-Muslim identity and the cultivation of religious morals.1

## The Architecture of Learning Pathways in the Southern Border Provinces

The educational ecosystem in the SBP is characterized by institutional plurality, where families frequently make strategic choices between religious and secular instruction at various stages of a child's development. This pluralism, while culturally rich, creates significant fragmentation as students move between systems that lack standardized credit transfer mechanisms.1

### Mainstream Public Schools and the State Mandate

Public schools under the Office of the Basic Education Commission (OBEC) serve as the primary vehicle for the Thai government’s "Education for All" policy.8 These schools are mandated to follow the national curriculum, focusing on Thai language proficiency and academic standards aligned with the Ordinary National Educational Test (O-NET).1 In the SBP, public schools often struggle with cultural relevance, as the curriculum is frequently perceived as failing to address the linguistic and religious needs of the Malay-Muslim majority.1 Recent efforts have introduced bilingual Thai-Melayu programs in approximately 15 primary schools to bridge this gap, aiming to foster a more inclusive environment that respects local identity while maintaining national standards.4

### Private Islamic Schools and the Crisis of Dualism

Private Islamic schools, or Sekolah Agama Rakyat, are the preferred choice for a majority of Muslim parents in the Deep South.5 These institutions have largely transitioned from traditional boarding schools into formalized entities that offer a dual curriculum: eight subjects from the national secular curriculum and a comprehensive Islamic studies program.1 This dualism creates an intense academic burden for students, who often face longer school hours and an overloaded timetable to accommodate both mandates.7 Consequently, student performance on national tests like the O-NET is consistently below the national average, raising concerns about the efficacy of this "integration" model.1

### Traditional Pondoks and Foundation Learning

The traditional pondok remains a foundational institution for religious refinement and spiritual leadership.2 These schools typically focus on classical Islamic texts, Arabic, and Jawi (Malay in Arabic script), operating with significant independence from state oversight.1 While many pondoks have registered as private schools, several hundred continue to operate as purely religious centers.2 At the earliest level, the Tadika system—mosque-based training centers for preschool and primary-aged children—provides the first exposure to Islamic morals and basic literacy for nearly 1,600 communities, serving as a critical cultural entry point.3

### Non-Formal and Informal Learning Mechanisms

For youth who have disconnected from formal schooling, the Non-Formal Education (NFE) system, locally known as Kor Sor Nor, provides a vital "second chance" pathway.12 NFE offers equivalency programs (N-NET) and vocational training that allow learners to obtain primary or secondary certificates through flexible, community-based instruction.13 Additionally, community learning centers and NGO-led programs, such as those coordinated by the Southern Border Provinces Administrative Centre (SBPAC) and UNICEF, focus on life skills, peace education, and vocational reintegration for vulnerable youth.15

## Transition Bottlenecks and the Risk of Systemic Disconnection

The points at which children and youth switch between these pathways are the most vulnerable stages for educational dropout or "invisibility." The research identifies several critical transition failures that occur due to linguistic barriers, socio-economic pressures, and the disconnect between religious and secular standards.2

### The Grade 6 Transition: From Secular to Religious Focus

One of the most significant shifts occurs after Grade 6, when many students leave public primary schools to enroll in private Islamic schools or traditional pondoks.2 This transition is often driven by parental expectations that children gain religious grounding before entering adulthood.5 However, the shift frequently involves a change in the language of instruction and a sudden increase in curriculum volume, leading to academic fatigue and a decline in secular competency.1

### The Upper Secondary Dropout Crisis

The transition from lower to upper secondary (Grade 9 to Grade 10) represents a peak period for educational attrition. In the SBP, out-of-school rates at the upper secondary level reach 20–21%—significantly higher than the 15% national average.18 This dropout is fueled by the economic necessity for youth to enter the informal labor market, often in unskilled roles within the agricultural sector or across the border in Malaysia.18 For male youth, the decision to leave school is also influenced by the perception that a high school diploma offers limited career advantages in a region with high unemployment and persistent security concerns.22

### Cross-Border Migration and "Out of Sight" Youth

The proximity of the SBP to Malaysia creates a unique "out of sight" phenomenon, where youth migrate for work or religious education in Malaysian madrasahs.20 Because there is no formal mechanism for tracking the education of these cross-border migrants, they often fall out of the Thai welfare and education registry.20 These youth frequently lack legal documentation in Malaysia, placing them at high risk of exploitation and self-imposed "house arrest" to avoid detection by immigration authorities.25 The lack of credit transfer between Thai and Malaysian systems means that even if these youth return to Thailand, their learning in Malaysia is rarely recognized.12

### Barriers to Re-entry and Reintegration

For youth who have dropped out, re-entering the formal system is hampered by administrative and psychological barriers.23 The requirement for a 13-digit identification code can be a deterrent for undocumented or migrant children, although the "G-code" system exists to circumvent this.8 However, the verification process for G-codes at the local district level remains cumbersome, and many families are unaware of these inclusionary policies.8 Furthermore, the stigma associated with NFE can prevent youth from viewing it as a viable pathway to higher education or skilled employment.28

## Structural Barriers to Competency Recognition

A central challenge in the SBP is that learning in religious or alternative settings frequently fails to convert into recognized credentials that facilitate social mobility. These barriers are rooted in pedagogical differences, assessment frameworks, and institutional fragmentation.1

### Pedagogical Misalignment

Traditional Islamic education in the Deep South emphasizes spiritual refinement, rote memorization of classical texts, and the authority of the teacher.1 This stands in contrast to the state's modern pedagogical push toward "Lesson Study" and "Open Approach" models, which prioritize critical thinking, student-centered learning, and ICT integration.1 Teachers in Islamic schools, many of whom lack formal pedagogical training or state certification, often struggle to bridge these two worlds.1 Without formal certification, the instruction provided in pondoks remains uncredited in the eyes of the Ministry of Education.1

### High-Stakes Assessments and Performance Gaps

The reliance on standardized tests like the O-NET creates a barrier for students in integrated schools who must divide their focus between two full curricula.1 While the B-NET exists to assess Islamic knowledge, its recognition by secular universities and employers is limited.7 Students from private Islamic schools consistently underperform on the O-NET, which limits their access to prestigious higher education programs and perpetuates a cycle of educational inequality.1

### Fragmentation of Governance

Responsibility for Islamic education in Thailand is "scattered" across various agencies, including the Office of Private Education Commission (OPEC) and regional educational management centers.3 This fragmentation leads to a lack of unified monitoring and standardization, making it difficult to establish clear equivalency frameworks for religious subjects.7 In contrast to the Bangsamoro region in the Philippines, where the Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE) has consolidated oversight to ensure that Islamic studies are a recognized component of the mainstream system, Thailand’s approach remains compartmentalized.7

## Vulnerability and the Care-Education Nexus

Educational pathways are further disrupted for the region’s most vulnerable populations: orphans and youth from households with high caregiving burdens. In the SBP, vulnerability is not only a result of poverty but is exacerbated by the chronic instability of the conflict.15

### The Trauma of Conflict-Related Orphanhood

The ongoing unrest has left thousands of children without primary breadwinners. Organizations like the Education for Development Foundation (EDF) have supported over 5,600 orphans in the three southernmost provinces through specialized scholarships like "Under the Same Sky".34 Despite this support, these children face heightened risks of mental health problems, including depression and anxiety, which are strongly correlated with truancy and dropout.23 The atmosphere of restricted mobility and the threat of violence further isolate these adolescents, limiting their social development and future aspirations.23

### The Burden of Home-Based Care

The conflict has also resulted in a high number of civilian casualties, many of whom require long-term care.35 In households where a member has been injured, the caregiving burden often falls on youth, particularly girls.35 This "care burden" is a significant predictor of educational discontinuity:

- Time Constraints: Youth spend significant hours assisting with activities of daily living (ADL), reducing time for schooling and homework.36
    
- Financial Stress: The loss of income from injured family members forces youth to drop out to pursue unskilled labor.37
    
- Psychological Fatigue: The stress of caregiving, combined with the lack of secondary caregivers in many poor families, diminishes the "resilience" of the youth, making them more likely to disengage from learning.36
    

### Stunting and Foundational Learning Skills

Data from the 2022 Multiple Indicator Cluster Survey (MICS) reveals that children in the South face critical disadvantages in health and early learning.19 Stunting rates in Ranong, Yala, Pattani, and Narathiwat are approximately 20–26%, nearly double the national average.19 This malnutrition is directly linked to poorer basic literacy and numeracy: in Narathiwat, only 15% of children in Grades 2 and 3 possess basic reading skills, compared to 47% nationally.19 These early childhood disadvantages create a "compounding deficit" that makes subsequent transitions between learning pathways even more difficult.15

## Comparative Insights: Models of Integration and Recognition

The challenges facing the SBP are mirrored in other Muslim-majority or multi-system contexts in Southeast Asia. Analyzing the Bangsamoro region in the Philippines and the integrated models in Malaysia provides plausible pathways for reform in Thailand.7

### The Bangsamoro Portfolio Assessment Model

In the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region (BARMM), the Alternative Learning System (ALS) has been instrumental in reintegrating youth whose education was interrupted by conflict.28 A key innovation is the Presentation Portfolio Assessment (PPA), which allows learners to demonstrate mastery of competencies through a compilation of work samples, life experience documentation, and prior learning records.39 This system recognizes that learning occurs outside the classroom—in religious schools or through workplace experience—and provides a formal accreditation pathway that does not rely solely on a single high-stakes exam.40

### The Integrated Islamic Education (IIE) Model in Malaysia

Malaysia has developed a more robust policy framework for integrating religious and secular subjects, focusing on teacher development to bridge the gap between spiritual and scientific knowledge.7 While the pedagogical implementation still faces challenges, the centralization of monitoring under agencies like the Office of Local District Education (OLDE) ensures that graduates from integrated schools are qualified for national tertiary education.7 Malaysia’s model suggests that successful integration requires moving beyond simply "splitting the timetable" to a more cohesive harmonization of subjects.7

## Plausible Models for Competency Recognition in the SBP

Addressing the fragmentation of learning pathways in the Southern Border Provinces requires innovative models that recognize the region's specific religious and cultural assets while ensuring alignment with national standards.5

### Model 1: The Integrated Credit Bank and Portfolio Recognition

This model, inspired by the Bangsamoro PPA, would establish a provincial "Credit Bank" managed by the SBPAC and regional education authorities.7

- Mechanism: Youth in traditional pondoks or NFE could maintain a learning portfolio that includes evidence of religious mastery (e.g., Arabic proficiency, jurisprudence knowledge) and practical vocational skills.40
    
- Recognition: These portfolios would be assessed by a joint panel of state educators and religious leaders (Tok Gurus). Upon successful validation, students would receive credits that are transferable to formal vocational colleges or state schools, effectively bridging the gap between unaccredited religious studies and formal diplomas.7
    

### Model 2: The Hybrid Teacher-Resident Program

To address the barrier of teacher certification, the state could implement a residency model for religious teachers in private Islamic schools.1

- Mechanism: Instead of requiring teachers to leave their schools for training, "master teachers" from universities like Prince of Songkla University (PSU) would conduct classroom-based professional development using the "Lesson Study" and "Open Approach" methods.1
    
- Recognition: Teachers who complete this residency and demonstrate the ability to integrate secular competencies (like critical thinking and ICT) into religious instruction would receive an "Integrated Instructor Certification," allowing their courses to be officially accredited by the Ministry of Education.1
    

### Model 3: The Cross-Border Educational Passport

Given the high rate of migration to Malaysia, a bilateral "Educational Passport" could be established under the ASEAN framework.12

- Mechanism: This digital record would track the educational progress of youth moving between Thailand and Malaysia, capturing certificates from both Thai NFE and Malaysian madrasahs.12
    
- Recognition: A joint commission would establish equivalency tables for religious and vocational subjects between the two countries, ensuring that a youth who spends three years in a Malaysian school can return to Thailand and re-enter the formal system at the appropriate grade level without losing academic credit.12
    

## Conclusion: Toward a Seamless and Resilient Ecosystem

The connectivity of learning pathways for children and youth in the Southern Border Provinces is currently hampered by a "dualism" that forces a choice between cultural identity and state-recognized success.1 The structural bottlenecks at the Grade 6 and upper secondary levels are not merely administrative failures but are symptomatic of a system that does not yet fully value the informal and religious knowledge prevalent in the region.1

To ensure that youth do not become "out of sight" or "out of reach," the Thai government must move toward a more modular and inclusive framework of competency recognition.3 This involves shifting away from high-stakes standardized testing as the sole gatekeeper of credentials and toward a more flexible assessment of learning through portfolios and prior learning recognition.7 Strengthening the resilience of vulnerable children—through trauma-informed care, nutritional support, and flexible NFE pathways—is equally critical for bridging the gap between poverty and opportunity.15

Ultimately, the future of education in the Deep South lies in the successful synthesis of the region’s traditional spiritual foundation with the competencies required for a modern professional life.5 By fostering collaboration between the state, religious leaders, and international partners like UNICEF, the SBP can transition from a fragmented system of parallel paths into a unified, seamless learning ecosystem that honors the identity of every child while opening doors to global opportunities.3
