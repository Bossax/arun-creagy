
# The Nusantara Horizon: A Futures Report on Youth Economic Integration in Thailand’s Southern Border Provinces to 2047

The Southern Border Provinces (SBP) of Thailand—comprising Pattani, Yala, Narathiwat, and parts of Songkhla and Satun—occupy a distinctive socio-spatial position within the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN). As the year 2047 approaches, marking over a century of modern state administration and nearly half a century since the resurgence of localized conflict, the region stands at a critical juncture. The youth of these provinces, primarily Malay-speaking Muslims who identify with the broader cultural and religious fabric of the Nusantara region, represent both the greatest potential and the most significant challenge for regional stability and economic development. The economic future of these young people is no longer confined to the binary of local agricultural subsistence or precarious migration; rather, it is being reshaped by a complex interplay of regional geopolitics, digital transformation, and the burgeoning global halal economy.

## The Socio-Economic Context of the Deep South

The Southern Border Provinces are characterized by a unique demographic profile where roughly 80 percent of the population is Muslim, predominantly of Malay ethnicity.1 This cultural distinctiveness is reflected in the linguistic landscape, where the local Malay dialect (Nayu) serves as the primary mode of communication, creating a natural affinity with neighboring Malaysia and Indonesia.2 For decades, this identity has fostered a sense of belonging to the "Nusantara"—the vast Malay world encompassing Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei, the southern Philippines, and Southern Thailand. However, this cultural richness has historically existed in tension with the centralized Thai state, leading to socio-economic marginalization, infrastructure deficits, and a persistent cycle of unrest.4

Historically, the economic baseline for youth in the SBP has been defined by low-value activities. The agricultural sector, dominated by rubber and palm oil, remains vulnerable to global price fluctuations and lacks the value-added processing necessary to create high-income jobs.6 Industrialization has been limited by security concerns and a perceived lack of alignment between national educational standards and local market needs.4 Consequently, migration has emerged not as a choice, but as a survival strategy. The "Tom Yam" restaurant network in Malaysia has for generations provided a vent for surplus labor, yet it remains largely informal and precarious.2

As Thailand looks toward 2047, the strategic objective is to shift the economic axis of the SBP from this precarious, low-value state to an upgraded, culturally rooted, and digitally connected halal/creative economy. This transition requires a deep understanding of the regional trends that are currently redefining the Nusantara.

## Key Regional Trends Shaping the 2047 Horizon

The economic trajectory of SBP youth is influenced by five dominant regional trends. These trends act as the structural drivers of the economic axis, determining the availability of work, the quality of life, and the degree of regional integration.

### Trend 1: The Evolution of Cross-Border Labor Migration Regimes

Labor migration from the SBP to Malaysia is the most significant informal economic link in the region. An estimated 100,000 workers from the five southern provinces are currently employed in Malaysia’s service sector, primarily in Tom Yam restaurants.2 These establishments rely on a constant supply of Muslim workers from Southern Thailand to ensure culinary authenticity and religious compliance (Halal).3

This migration pattern matters for SBP youth because it represents a "double-edged sword." While it provides immediate income and escapes local unemployment, the irregular status of most workers traps them in a cycle of vulnerability. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted these risks, as border closures forced many workers into undocumented status, excluded them from government aid, and led to mass arrests in immigration centers.9 By 2047, the professionalization and legalization of this labor flow—shifting from informal "border pass" workers to certified hospitality professionals—is essential for economic upgrading.9

### Trend 2: The IMT-GT Framework and Subregional Connectivity

The Indonesia-Malaysia-Thailand Growth Triangle (IMT-GT) serves as the primary institutional vehicle for subregional integration. Covering 14 provinces in Southern Thailand, 11 states in Malaysia, and 10 provinces in Sumatra, the IMT-GT aims to leverage complementarities to boost growth in disadvantaged border areas.11

The IMT-GT Vision 2036 and the Implementation Blueprint (IB) 2022–2026 provide the roadmap for this integration, focusing on eight strategic pillars, including halal products, digital transformation, and human resource development.11 A key development is the reconfiguration of economic corridors. Corridor 6, which incorporates Johor, Pahang, and Terengganu in Malaysia alongside the SBP, is designed to facilitate seamless trade and investment.15 For youth, this means a transition from isolated local markets to a connected subregional economy where MSMEs can participate in regional value chains through the IMT-GT E-commerce Platform and the "IMT-GT Mall".15

### Trend 3: National Strategic Visions and the Halal Imperative

The three core nations of the Nusantara—Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia—have launched ambitious long-term visions that place the halal economy at the center of their national growth strategies. Malaysia’s "Malaysia Madani" philosophy and its Halal Industry Master Plan (HIMP) 2030 aim to position the country as a global halal hub, contributing RM231 billion to GDP by 2030.17 Similarly, the "Indonesia Emas 2045" vision aims to escape the middle-income trap by 2038 and reach top-10 global economy status by 2045, with a focus on sustainable and inclusive growth.19

Thailand has countered with its own strategic push to become the "Halal Hub of ASEAN," establishing the Thai Halal Industry Center and the National Halal Industry Committee.20 The global halal market is projected to reach $5 trillion by 2030, covering food, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, and tourism.17 For SBP youth, these visions represent a massive opportunity. The region is a primary source of raw materials and has a workforce naturally attuned to halal standards. Integrating SBP youth into these national visions can transform them from "migrant workers" to "halal entrepreneurs" who bridge the gap between Thai production and Nusantara consumption.20

### Trend 4: Digital Transformation and the Creative Economy

Digitalization is decoupling economic opportunity from geographic location. Thailand’s "Thailand 4.0" strategy and its focus on digital entrepreneurship have created an environment where internet penetration is reaching 84%.23 In the SBP, this trend is manifesting through the rise of digital marketing for local products and the emergence of "digital nomads" and remote service providers.24

The creative economy, which includes fashion, cultural tourism, and Thai food, is already a significant driver, contributing 10% to Thailand’s GDP.30 For SBP youth, the fusion of their religious identity with creative technology offers a pathway to high-value work that remains "rooted" in their local community while serving a global audience.29

### Trend 5: Cross-Border Education and Human Capital Development

The final trend is the emergence of regional education networks that bypass national silos. The ASEAN University Network (AUN) and initiatives like the DEFFUUZ Consortium—involving Fatoni University in Thailand, along with partners in Malaysia, Indonesia, and Bangladesh—are pioneering dual-degree programs and joint research on digital innovation and sustainability.32

These pathways allow SBP youth to gain credentials that are recognized across the border, facilitating "brain circulation" rather than just permanent "brain drain".32 Programs like the International Student Mobility Program at Prince of Songkla University (PSU) Pattani campus foster an international campus environment that prepares youth for the multicultural realities of the Nusantara.36 This trend is critical for 2047, as it builds the human capital necessary to manage the upgraded halal and creative sectors.37

## Effects on Youth Education and Work Choices

The convergence of these regional trends is fundamentally altering the incentives and trade-offs faced by youth in the Southern Border Provinces. For decades, the incentive to migrate to Malaysia was driven by a stark wage differential—where wages for comparable roles are three to four times higher across the border—and a local economy that offered few opportunities for those with a Malay-Muslim identity.6 However, as we look toward 2047, the "work choice" is becoming more nuanced.

The emerging "halal/creative economy" provides an incentive for youth to stay, or at least to engage in "circular migration." Work in Tom Yam restaurants is being supplemented, and in some cases replaced, by roles in halal food processing, community-based halal tourism, and digital entrepreneurship.4 Youth are increasingly seeing the value of combining their religious knowledge with technical credentials. This has led to a demand for "identity-affirming" education—models that integrate Thai, Malay, and English as equal pillars.39 Such an education prepares students for legal literacy in Thailand while maintaining the linguistic and cultural trust necessary to do business in Malaysia and Indonesia.39

The shift is also visible in vocational training. There is a burgeoning interest in specialized certificates—such as halal food science, Islamic finance, and digital marketing—that allow youth to access higher-value segments of the regional market.23 As institutions like PSU and Fatoni University expand their cross-border partnerships, youth can pursue "dual-degree" pathways where they spend time in both Thai and Malaysian or Indonesian institutions, gaining the "Nusantara competency" that will be the hallmark of the 2047 workforce.33

## Effects on the Economic Axis and Scenario Design

The economic future of the SBP can be envisioned through two divergent scenarios, determined by the quality of regional integration and the ability of stakeholders to leverage the SBP's cultural assets.

### Scenario A: The “Labour Exporter” World (Unfavourable Integration)

In a future defined by hostile politics, tighter border controls, and a failure to harmonize halal standards, the SBP remains trapped in a low-value, precarious state. In this scenario:

- Persistent Informality: The majority of youth continue to migrate to Malaysia as irregular workers, vulnerable to legal crackdowns and exploitation.9
    
- Brain Drain: The most talented youth leave the region permanently for urban centers like Kuala Lumpur or Bangkok, as local opportunities remain stagnant.2
    
- Stunted Growth: The region remains a primary producer of raw rubber and palm oil, with much of the value-added benefit accruing to processing hubs in Central Thailand or Malaysia.6
    
- Economic Isolation: Despite growth in the wider Nusantara, the SBP is bypassed by major corridors due to security concerns and "thick" borders, leading to localized poverty and continued unrest.5
    

### Scenario B: The “Rooted Hub” World (Favourable Integration)

Conversely, a future of favorable integration—marked by real labor protections, robust halal trade links, and cross-border educational recognition—shifts the SBP to the "upgraded" end of the economic axis. In this scenario:

- Value-Added Hub: The SBP becomes a regional center for halal food processing, pharmaceuticals, and cosmetics, leveraging local materials and skilled youth labor.20
    
- Digital Connectivity: Youth engage in cross-border digital trade and service work, remaining rooted in their home provinces while participating in the $5 trillion regional halal market.22
    
- Educational Excellence: SBP universities are recognized as global leaders in Islamic sciences and halal technology, attracting students and investment from across the Nusantara.34
    
- Shared Prosperity: The region functions as a "bridgehead" for Thailand’s entry into the Muslim markets of ASEAN, resulting in balanced development, improved social cohesion, and lasting peace.1

## Empirical Foundations of the SBP Economic Axis

The scenarios described above are grounded in existing empirical work that highlights the specific challenges and opportunities for SBP youth.

### Southern Thai Labour in Malaysia

Research by Bunmak (2012, 2013) and others has meticulously documented the "Tom Yam" economy.3 This work shows that migration is driven by a lack of local industrial demand and is facilitated by dense social networks based on ethnic and religious ties.2 These networks are resilient; they provide the "financial, accommodation, and transportation" support that allows youth to navigate a foreign labor market even without formal legal status.8 However, this informality is a structural weakness. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the lack of a bilateral labor agreement meant that Thai workers were often the first to be laid off and the last to receive assistance, highlighting the urgent need for a shift toward more formalized migration regimes.9

### Regional Halal Value Chains

The development of regional halal value chains is already underway, but the SBP's participation remains below its potential. Thai halal exports represent only 1.6% of the global market, with food accounting for 67% of that value.20 Empirical studies suggest that for the SBP to move up the value chain, there must be a shift toward "Halal Logistics" and "Innovation Solutions".44 This involves using technologies like blockchain to maintain the authenticity of certifications and developing "Halal Industrial Parks" that can attract investment from neighboring Malaysia and Indonesia.20 The integration of "Creative Economy" principles—such as branding and digital storytelling—is also vital for differentiating SBP halal products in a competitive global market.29

### Education and Training Cooperation

Specific cooperation programs, such as the Networking Programme between Thai Youths from SBP and Thai Students in Malaysia launched by the Royal Thai Embassy in Kuala Lumpur, are beginning to build the professional networks needed for a "Rooted Hub" future.42 These initiatives provide youth with exposure to the Thai private sector in Malaysia and help develop the career skills necessary for cross-border trade.42 Furthermore, the work of the SEAMEO STEM-ED Centre and the DEFFUUZ Consortium highlights the move toward "AI for Green Sustainability" and "innovation-driven poverty alleviation" as core components of SBP youth education.34

## Geopolitical Dynamics: The Thailand–Malaysia–Indonesia Nexus

The realization of a "Rooted Hub" world is contingent on the geopolitical alignment of the three neighbors. The relationship between Thailand and Malaysia is the most immediate pivot point. For years, this relationship was viewed primarily through the lens of security and border control. However, the "Malaysia Madani" approach and Thailand's "Quick-Big-Win" policy for the halal industry suggest a shift toward economic collaboration.18 By focusing on "shared prosperity" in the border regions, both nations can narrow the development gaps that have fueled past instability.41

Indonesia’s role is equally crucial. As the largest economy in the Nusantara and the world's most populous Muslim-majority nation, Indonesia is a primary market and an ideological anchor for SBP youth. The "Nusantara 2045" vision, while physically centered on a new capital, serves as a symbol of regional rebirth and innovation.31 If the SBP can be positioned as the northern gateway to this burgeoning Nusantara market, the economic incentives for youth to engage in high-value, cross-border trade will become overwhelming.

## Conclusion: The Path to 2047

The Southern Border Provinces of Thailand are at a historical threshold. The youth of today, who navigate between their Thai citizenship and their Nusantara identity, are the architects of the 2047 reality. The transition from a "Labour Exporter" world to a "Rooted Hub" world is not a foregone conclusion; it requires a deliberate and sustained effort to:

1. Professionalize the Border: Shifting from informal, precarious migration to regulated, professional labor mobility that protects youth rights.9
    
2. Scale the Halal Industry: Moving from raw materials to high-value, digitally-traceable halal products that leverage SBP cultural authenticity.20
    
3. Reform Education: Adopting identity-affirming, trilingual education models that produce the "Nusantara-competent" professionals of the future.34
    
4. Harness the Digital Frontier: Empowering youth entrepreneurs to use regional platforms and creative technologies to drive growth while remaining rooted in their communities.22
    

If these strategies are successfully implemented, the Southern Border Provinces will no longer be a peripheral zone defined by conflict, but a central node of regional prosperity—a rooted hub where youth can stay, thrive, and lead the Nusantara into a new century.
