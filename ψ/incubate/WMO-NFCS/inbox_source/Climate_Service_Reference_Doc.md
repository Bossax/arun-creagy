## 1. Defining "Climate Service"
The sources move away from a "product-only" view, defining a service as a dynamic, interactive system.

### Core Definitions
*   **The Global Standard (GFCS/WMO)**: "Climate information prepared and delivered to meet users’ needs." 
*   **Functional Definition**: "A service providing climate information in a way that assists decision-making by individuals and organizations."
*   **The Systems View**: A "complex combination of data, processes, products, actors, sources of knowledge, delivery modes, and organizations." This ensures that knowledge is not just scientifically robust but "fit for purpose" for specific decisions.
*   **The Process View**: The overarching process of generating, providing, and using climate information to assist in decision-making.

### The "Service" Threshold
A critical takeaway from the *NFCS Guidelines* is that **raw climate information is not a climate service**. It only crosses the threshold to become a "service" when it involves:
1.  **Appropriate Engagement**: Iterative dialogue between provider and user.
2.  **Effective Access Mechanisms**: Reliable ways for users to retrieve and use the info.
3.  **Direct Response to Needs**: The content must be shaped by the user's specific problem, not just the provider's available data.

## 2. Distinction: Data vs. Products vs. Services
This hierarchy is essential for explaining the "Value-Add" of climate services in workshop settings.

| Category | Definition | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Climate Data** | Raw historical/real-time observations and model outputs. | Foundational; requires metadata; often not readily usable by non-scientists. |
| **Climate Products** | A "derived synthesis" of data. | Integrates multidisciplinary knowledge; adds value; meets broad societal needs (e.g., a general map). |
| **Climate Services** | The interactive delivery of products/info to support specific decisions. | **Iterative**; **Co-produced**; **Tailored**; **Actionable**. |

### Key Differentiators (The "Service" DNA)
1.  **Iterative User Engagement**: Unlike a product (which is often "pushed" to a user), a service is **co-produced**. It requires partnership-building and testing/refining based on client feedback.
2.  **Tailoring and Translation**: A service blends climate info with **sector-specific knowledge**. 
    *   *Example*: A 3-month rainfall forecast is **Information**. Overlaying that forecast with crop phenology and soil moisture to tell a farmer when to plant is a **Climate Service** (Agromet Advisory).
3.  **Decision-Support ("Fit for Purpose")**: Services actively translate temporal and spatial data into tools that fit a *particular* decision-making context (e.g., local infrastructure planning vs. national food security).
4.  **Routine Delivery**: A service is an ongoing process with emphasis on how information is represented and communicated so users can apply it confidently in daily operations.

## 3. Development Roadmaps (How to Build)
The sources identify three distinct methodologies depending on the scale of the project.

### A. The Institutional Roadmap (NFCS 5-Step Process)
*Recommended for national-level framework design.*
1.  **Assessing the Baseline**: Map the "national chain" (users, providers, boundary orgs) and identify capacity gaps.
2.  **Stakeholder Consultation**: A co-design workshop to build consensus on urgent needs and joint actions.
3.  **Strategic & Action Planning**: Translating workshop results into a costed roadmap with a "shared vision" for delivery.
4.  **Endorsement & Validation**: High-level political validation and resource mobilization (funding).
5.  **Launch & Implementation**: Executing the plan and establishing **M&E (Monitoring & Evaluation)** to track performance and impact.

### B. The Technical Production Chain (Example: Norway/NCCS)
*Recommended for localized, data-heavy service design.*
1.  **Data Production**: Downscaling and bias-adjusting Global/Regional models to local scales (e.g., 1x1 km resolution).
2.  **Dissemination & Co-production**: Translating raw data into "Climate Factsheets" and APIs in collaboration with local planners.

### C. The Risk Assessment Workflow (European Commission/CLARITY)
*Recommended for hazard-specific services (e.g., Heat/Flood).*
1.  Characterize Hazard.
2.  Evaluate Exposure.
3.  Analyze Vulnerability.
4.  Assess Risk and Impact.
5.  Identify Adaptation Options.
6.  Appraise Adaptation Options.
7.  Implement/Integrate Adaptation Action Plan.

## 4. Contextual Notes on Methodology
*   **Co-design vs. Consultation**: True services go beyond "consulting" users; they involve "joint visioning" and "revised problem definitions."
*   **IT as a Bridge**: In urban settings, climate services are often viewed as IT tools that bridge the gap between complex climate science and operational Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR).
*   **Success Metrics**: The success of a climate service is judged by its **usefulness, usability, and actual usage** in supporting decision-making.
