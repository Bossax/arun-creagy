The architectural integrity of national environmental portals serves as a critical determinant in the efficacy of climate governance. In the context of Thailand’s ambitious climate commitments, the transition of the Department of Environmental Quality Promotion (DEQP) into the Department of Climate Change and Environment (DCCE) on August 18, 2023, represents more than a rebranding effort; it signifies a structural pivot toward data-centric climate management. This transition, mandated by a Royal Decree, was designed specifically to consolidate Thailand’s efforts in addressing the climate crisis, enhancing the capacity of personnel, and promoting multi-agency collaboration. The digital manifestation of this mandate is the official DCCE website ([https://www.dcce.go.th/](https://www.dcce.go.th/)), which currently functions as the primary nexus for policy dissemination, greenhouse gas (GHG) reporting, and public engagement.

To ensure this digital infrastructure is prepared for the upcoming National Climate AI and Information Framework (NCAIF) vNext, a rigorous inventory of the current site is required. This analysis explores the navigation hierarchies, specialized information systems, and policy clusters to identify existing strengths and critical gaps. The following evaluation considers the department's role as a Non-Annex I Party under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), obligated to provide transparent data regarding National Communications (NC), Biennial Update Reports (BUR), and the newly established Biennial Transparency Reports (BTR).

## The Central Information Center: Policy and Legal Foundations

The "Central Information Center" (ศูนย์ข้อมูลกลาง) is perhaps the most critical cluster for policy analysts and international observers. It serves as the repository for Thailand's climate commitments and the legislative tools developed to achieve them. This section is not merely a document host but a strategic map of Thailand's path toward international compliance under the Paris Agreement.

### Legislative Frameworks and National Strategies

The center features the Draft Climate Change Act, which is intended to serve as the central legal framework for climate action in the country. This act is supported by the National Climate Change Policy Committee (กนภ.), which oversees national policies and strategies. The digital presentation of these documents is vital for ensuring that both the public and private sectors understand their legal obligations under the new regime.

Furthermore, the center provides access to the National Climate Change Adaptation Plan and the National Greenhouse Gas Reduction Action Plan. These documents are the operational blueprints for Thailand's mitigation and resilience efforts. By hosting these centrally, the DCCE ensures that all stakeholders have access to the same strategic data, reducing informational asymmetry.

### Insight into the Transition to BTR

The reporting framework for Thailand is currently shifting from the Biennial Update Report (BUR) to the Biennial Transparency Report (BTR) following the outcomes of COP24 in Katowice. This shift necessitates more rigorous data collection and reporting on national GHG inventory data, tracking progress toward Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) targets, and providing information on adaptation and support received. The DCCE website’s role in this transition is central, as it must facilitate the collection and compilation of data for GHG emissions in five key sectors: Energy and Transportation, Industrial Processes and Product Use (IPPU), Agriculture, Forestry and Other Land Use (AFOLU), and Waste.

## Technical Ecosystem: Specialized Information Systems (CCE Information)

The "CCE Information" (สารสนเทศ CCE) section represents the technical "engine room" of the DCCE's digital infrastructure. It consists of more than twenty specialized portals and databases designed for specific monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) activities. This ecosystem is highly granular, providing tools for sectors ranging from hospitality (Green Hotel) to urban planning (Green Area).

### The Primary Technical Hubs: CCIC and Risk MAP

At the core of the technical ecosystem are the Climate Change Information Center (CCIC) and the Spatial Risk Database System (Risk MAP). These systems are designed to provide the data necessary for evidence-based decision-making.

The CCIC functions as the primary data hub for climate-related statistics and forecasting tools. It is intended to consolidate GHG inventory information and provide a single point of access for government and civil society sectors to communicate and report data. In contrast, the Risk MAP system provides spatial data for assessing environmental and climate risks across different geographic areas, allowing for localized adaptation planning.

## Content Cluster Analysis: News and Public Engagement

The "News" and "Public Services" sections of the website serve as the primary interface between the department and the public. These sections are updated frequently, reflecting the active nature of the DCCE’s work. In early 2026, the department launched several key initiatives, including focus group meetings for long-term low-emission development strategies and training sessions on climate forecast data systems.

### News Categorization and Strategy

The DCCE categorizes its news to cater to different informational needs. This categorization ensures that technical project updates do not obscure general public relations news.

- **Public Relations News:** General updates, such as welcoming the "Miss Climate Change 2026" committee to discuss public awareness roles.
    
- **Project News:** Specific updates on climate and environmental projects, such as the selection for high-level environmental governance certificates.
    
- **CCE News:** Dedicated technical news regarding climate change and environment.
    
- **Procurement and Recruitment:** Administrative news regarding job vacancies and government tenders.
    

This active update cycle is essential for maintaining public trust and ensuring that stakeholders are aware of the department's progress toward the 2050/2065 targets. The inclusion of volunteer networks like the "DCCE News Hawks" further illustrates the department's commitment to community-level climate action.

### Public Services and e-Learning Infrastructure

The "Public Services" section includes a range of tools designed to foster capacity building. The Environmental Seminar and Training System and the e-Learning platform are critical for educating government officials and the public on the complexities of climate change.

Furthermore, the DCCE Application and the electronic media system (featuring a library of research reports, e-books, and multimedia content) provide mobile-accessible education. This multimedia approach is necessary for a diverse audience, ensuring that complex scientific data is translated into understandable formats for non-experts.

## Thailand Taxonomy: A Deep Dive into Green Financial Classification

A major addition to the DCCE’s digital content is the Thailand Taxonomy. This classification system is designed to separate sustainable activities from those that are harmful to the environment. The taxonomy serves as a tool for the government to direct capital flows toward projects that contribute to the country’s climate objectives.

### The Three Core Pillars and the Traffic Light System

The Thailand Taxonomy is built upon three core pillars that determine whether an activity is aligned with sustainable goals:

1. **Substantial Contribution:** The activity must contribute to at least one of the six environmental objectives (Mitigation, Adaptation, Water, Circular Economy, Pollution Control, Biodiversity).
    
2. **Do No Significant Harm (DNSH):** The activity must not adversely impact any of the other five environmental objectives.
    
3. **Minimum Social Safeguards (MSS):** The activity must comply with basic social and labor standards to prevent negative social consequences.
    

The "Traffic Light System" provides a clear visual indicator for investors:

- **Green:** Activities that clearly and significantly contribute to environmental objectives.
    
- **Amber:** Transitional activities that are on a decarbonization pathway but have not yet met full green criteria.
    
- **Red:** Activities that are unsustainable and harmful.
    

### Sectoral Focus and Implementation

The taxonomy Phase I and Phase II prioritize six sectors that are major contributors to environmental impact: Energy, Transportation, Construction & Real Estate, Manufacturing, Waste Management, and Agriculture. For example, in the construction sector, the taxonomy evaluates performance using emission per square meter (carbon intensity), covering Scope 1 direct emissions and energy use during a building's lifetime.

This level of detail is critical for the NCAIF vNext, as it provides the structured data needed for AI to evaluate corporate sustainability disclosures. Currently, this information is primarily available in PDF formats or webinars on the DCCE site. Integrating this into an interactive, database-driven tool would be a major leap forward for Thailand's green finance ecosystem.

## Content Gap Analysis against NCAIF Sitemap vNext

When the current DCCE website inventory is compared against the proposed NCAIF vNext framework, several structural and functional gaps become apparent. The vNext model moves away from a "portal of portals" approach toward a unified, AI-ready data environment.

### Identified Gaps and Strategic Deficiencies

The primary gap is the lack of integration across the department's specialized portals. While systems like "Data Trash" and "Green Area" collect valuable data, they operate as standalone entities. The NCAIF vNext requires a **Unified Climate Data Lake** where these datasets can be cross-referenced to identify synergies—such as the impact of urban greening on local temperature and energy use.

|**Gap Area**|**Current State (DCCE)**|**Requirement (NCAIF vNext)**|**Gap Analysis**|
|---|---|---|---|
|Data Integration|Fragmented subdomains and siloed portals.|Centralized API-first architecture and unified data lake.|High: Current silos prevent holistic AI-driven analysis.|
|Reporting Lag|Inventory data typically lags by several years (e.g., 2021 data reported in 2026).|Real-time or near-real-time emission tracking and NDC dashboards.|Medium: Requires automated data feeds from sectors.|
|Search Capability|Standard keyword-based search for documents and news.|Semantic search and AI Knowledge Base for policy navigation.|High: Users struggle to find specific regulations across portals.|

|Risk Assessment|Static "Risk MAP" tool based on spatial databases.|Dynamic predictive modeling using AI to forecast future vulnerabilities.|Medium: Data exists but requires advanced analytical layers.|

### Technical Limitations in GHG Inventory Management

Thailand currently faces limitations and gaps in preparing its GHG inventories, which affect transparency, accuracy, and completeness (TACCC). The DCCE is working to address these through a centralized digital platform for the national GHG database. However, the current inventory shows that this platform is still in a phase of improvement, with limitations in data quality across the five IPCC sectors.

The transition to BTR reporting requires a level of data granularity that the current website architecture is just beginning to support. For the Energy and Transportation sectors, the department is currently holding focus groups to improve calculation methods for emission factors. This indicates that the "vNext" sitemap must prioritize not just the _display_ of data, but the _tools_ for its collection and validation.

## Toward a Unified National Climate Information Framework

The inventory of the DCCE website reveals a robust foundation of policy documents, legislative drafts, and specialized monitoring tools. The transition from DEQP to DCCE has successfully created a "Central Information Center" that houses the country's climate strategy. However, the proliferation of specialized portals has led to a fragmented digital landscape that hinders the effective use of AI and advanced data analytics.

To align with the NCAIF vNext, the DCCE should focus on the following strategic evolutions:

1. **Portal Consolidation:** Migrating the twenty-plus specialized systems (Green Hotel, Eco School, etc.) into a single, authenticated dashboard that provides a 360-degree view of national sustainability progress.
    
2. **API Modernization:** Exposing the data within the CCIC and Risk MAP systems via secure APIs to allow researchers and developers to build AI models on top of official datasets.
    
3. **Interactive Taxonomy:** Converting the Thailand Taxonomy from a series of documents into a digital utility where companies can verify their alignment with "Green" and "Amber" criteria in real-time.
    
4. **Real-Time Dashboards:** Implementing live NDC tracking dashboards that move beyond periodic reporting toward active management of the country's carbon budget.
    

By addressing these gaps, the DCCE can transition its digital infrastructure from a repository of records into a dynamic engine for climate action. This will ensure that Thailand not only meets its international reporting obligations but also utilizes its data to drive the national transition toward a net-zero future. The current website represents a vital first step, but the "vNext" evolution will be defined by integration, intelligence, and real-time transparency.
