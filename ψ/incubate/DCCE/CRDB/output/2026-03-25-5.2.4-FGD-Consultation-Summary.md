## 1. Objectives and design of the consultations

### 1.1 FGD1 – Current landscape and internal needs

FGD1 was designed to clarify **what DCCE already has** in terms of systems, websites, datasets, reports, and platforms related to climate risk and adaptation, and **what internal users feel is missing**.

From the synthesis note, the main objectives were to:

- map existing assets (spatial risk map, adaptation-related reports, M&E systems, GHG inventory systems, information portals, and central data initiatives);
- elicit internal pain points around terminology, data quality, and access; and
- understand how staff in different divisions experience the current web and data landscape.

### 1.2 FGD2 – Expectations for NCAIF and Phase 1 outputs

FGD2 was held after preliminary NCAIF and CDM designs had been drafted. Its objectives were to:

- test the **sitemap options and NCAIF framing** with decision-makers and key stakeholders;
- clarify expectations for the **endorsed baseline registry** and the broader data catalog;
- align understanding about **Phase 1 scope** (logical architecture and specifications, not full system build); and
- confirm how the adaptation cycle should be reflected in the platform.

The action summary emphasises that leadership mentally equates “platform” with **website + adaptation information service**, so the consultations were used to test whether the proposed hybrid sitemap and MVP set made sense to them.

### 1.3 Other interviews and engagements

Beyond the two focus groups, the project uses interviews and working meetings with agencies such as **NXPO**, **NESDC**, **NSO**, sectoral ministries, city partners, and financial-sector actors as part of the broader consultation process. These are covered in detail in Chapter 2 and the interview/use-case artifacts, but they also influence the NCAIF structure and data-management design reflected in Section 1.

## 2. Participant profiles (types)

The synthesis note and action summary indicate three broad groups of participants:

1. **Internal DCCE staff** – from multiple divisions responsible for climate policy, risk assessment, adaptation programmes, GHG inventories, and information systems. They bring knowledge of existing assets and internal constraints.
2. **DCCE leadership and senior decision-makers** – who shape expectations about what “success” looks like in Phase 1 and what they want to see in the interim report and the platform structure.
3. **External partners and stakeholders** – including representatives from other ministries, agencies, and specialist organisations (covered more fully in Chapter 2), who provide use cases and requirements that NCAIF must support.

For TOR 5.2.4, the emphasis is on how FGD1 and FGD2, as DCCE-centric consultations, influenced NCAIF and CDM design.

## 3. Key signals from FGD1 (current-state reality)

FGD1 generates three main types of signals:

1. **Existing assets but fragmented structure.** Participants confirm that DCCE already operates or contributes to multiple systems and products: the spatial risk map, adaptation M&E initiatives, communication channels, GHG and inventory systems, and a developing central information centre. The problem is not the absence of data, but the lack of a coherent organizing logic across these assets.

2. **Terminology, data quality, and QA/QC issues.** Staff note that definitions of adaptation-related terms differ across units, datasets are often delayed or incomplete, and there is no shared, department-wide QA/QC standard. They call for common definitions, shared quality rules, and tiered information levels appropriate for different user groups.

3. **UX and workflow challenges.** Participants report that existing systems are difficult for non-technical users. Interfaces are not friendly, content is scattered, and there are few tools to preview data or guide interpretation. They suggest clearer dashboards, better summaries, and mechanisms for two-way communication (e.g. FAQ, help channels).

These signals underpin the v1 report’s conclusion that the current landscape is **resource-rich but structurally fragmented**, and that NCAIF must be designed as a coherent information service rather than another isolated site.

## 4. Key signals from FGD2 (expectations and constraints)

The FGD2 action summary distils several critical expectations that directly shape NCAIF and the data-management structure:

1. **Sitemap-first platform framing.** Decision-makers strongly focus on the sitemap. In their mental model, “website” and “information platform” are effectively the same thing. They expect to see a **baseline data inventory and an adaptation information website** as visible outputs. This reinforces the need to present CDM and data-governance content as the **back-end logic that enables the sitemap** rather than as a competing, abstract deliverable.

2. **Endorsed baseline registry as guidance, not just a list.** Stakeholders ask for a recommended/endorsed baseline registry that includes:
   - clear statements of what each baseline is **valid for / not valid for**;
   - visible last-updated/version information; and
   - simple language guidance for non-technical users.
   This shapes the fields and presentation of MVP-3 and the way the baseline registry appears in NCAIF.

3. **Multiple access rails for the data catalog.** FGD2 emphasises that the catalog must handle **open**, **G2G**, and **internal-only** datasets. This validates the publishing-rails stance (Open → data.go.th, G2G via GDX, internal for sensitive data) and requires the NCAIF catalog to distinguish between these rails rather than assuming everything becomes open data.

4. **Phase 1 as logical architecture and business requirements.** Participants accept that Phase 1 is about **logical architecture and specifications**, not full system implementation. They expect deliverables that can be used as **specifications and handoff materials** for later implementation projects, rather than a fully functional data platform.

5. **Adaptation cycle completeness.** FGD2 explicitly asks that the platform and report cover the full adaptation cycle: risk, planning, implementation, and M&E. This confirms that NCAIF’s backbone must extend beyond risk mapping to cover measures and monitoring, even if some components are currently linked out to other systems.

6. **Interest in automation and data flow.** Decision-makers express interest in how data “flows between boxes,” how far automation can go, and what can be handled by metadata versus later integration projects. This leads to the need for an explicit **automation roadmap** in the data-management structure note.

## 5. How consultations influenced NCAIF and CDM design

Taken together, FGD1 and FGD2 shape several design choices reflected in Section 1:

- They confirm that **NCAIF must integrate existing assets** rather than starting from scratch, which reinforces the catalog-first and link-first architectural stance.
- They push the project to settle on **Option 3 (hybrid, workflow-pattern-first) sitemap**, preserving the adaptation cycle and Policy Maker Centre while refining how subject areas and tools surface.
- They drive the definition of **MVP priorities** (MVP-3 baseline registry and MVP-2 disaster data ingestion and loss/damage groundwork), because these respond directly to demands for endorsed baselines and post-event information.
- They validate the need for **clear governance gates and publishing rails** to address trust, classification, and QA/QC concerns raised in FGD1.

For TOR 5.2.4, the key message is that the NCAIF and CDM designs are not abstract constructs: they are **grounded in concrete consultation signals** about current pain points, expectations for visibility, and the need for practical governance.
