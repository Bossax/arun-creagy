# Three-Platform Review and T-PLAT Lessons

## Purpose

## Review of the three benchmark platforms from the inception report

### 1. A-PLAT (Japan)

- Functions as a national adaptation information platform with clear pathways for central government, local government, private sector, and citizens.
- Combines policy and planning information with practical service layers such as the Climate & Impact Atlas, sector pages, local adaptation resources, and adaptation databases.
- Strongest contribution is translation: it makes technical climate and impact knowledge legible for different user groups without treating all users as the same.

### 2. KlimAdapt / KLiVO Portal (Germany)

- Functions less as a single content site and more as a curated access layer to climate preparedness services.
- Its service catalogue is the key design move: users filter by planning purpose, field of action, climate variable, climate impact, region, target group, and category.
- Strongest contribution is curation and quality control: inclusion criteria, metadata discipline, and update rules make the platform more than a repository of scattered links.

### 3. Climate-ADAPT (European Union)

- Functions as a multi-level knowledge hub linking EU policy, national and local profiles, data explorers, tools, indicators, case studies, and resource catalogues.
- Strongest contribution is scale management: it connects policy, geography, tools, and knowledge assets across levels rather than forcing everything into one simple menu tree.
- It shows how a platform can separate policy guidance, searchable resources, and specialist tools while still keeping them connected.

## What the three-platform review establishes

Taken together, the benchmark set shows that a climate adaptation platform is not just a document portal. It usually needs at least four layers working together:

1. **Policy and guidance layer** for mandates, plans, and explanatory framing.
2. **Service and tool layer** for maps, assessment tools, or guided analytical functions.
3. **Knowledge and case layer** for reports, case studies, examples, and learning materials.
4. **Curation and governance layer** for metadata, quality control, update responsibility, and pathways by user group.

This is the benchmark logic behind why the project moved away from treating the task as only a website-content exercise.

## T-PLAT in relation to the benchmark trio

T-PLAT matters because it shows that Thailand already attempted to translate this benchmark logic into a domestic adaptation platform. In the inception report, T-PLAT appears as a Thai effort influenced by A-PLAT and AP-PLAT, later accompanied by the more specific [`T-PLAT.INFO`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/inbox_source/260106_DCCE_Climate%20risk%20database_inception%20report_vfinal.pdf) pilot around coastal adaptation work.

What T-PLAT demonstrates:

- Thailand did not start from zero; the platform idea, audience logic, and adaptation-information framing were already tested.
- The Thai effort could already present reports and map-based risk information, so the value of site-based adaptation information was proven.
- But the effort remained closer to a project-bounded portal and pilot interface than to a durable national information architecture.

In other words, T-PLAT is best read as an important predecessor, but not yet as the structural answer to the current CRDB mandate.

## What we learn from T-PLAT

- **T-PLAT validates the platform direction.** NCAIF is not inventing the need for an adaptation platform; it is inheriting and refining an already tested direction.
- **T-PLAT shows the value of visible user-facing services.** Reports, maps, and accessible explanations matter because they make adaptation information publicly legible.
- **T-PLAT also shows the limits of portal-first design.** A platform can look useful at the interface level while still lacking the relational structure, metadata discipline, and governance needed for long-term integration.
- **T-PLAT was too project-dependent to become the national backbone.** It surfaced information, but it did not solve the wider problem of cross-project interoperability and managed integration across DCCE assets.
- **Therefore NCAIF must go further than replication.** The lesson is to keep the accessibility ambition of T-PLAT, but place it on top of CDM, metadata, governance, and workflow-based service design so the next platform is not only informative but structurally extensible.

## Drafting use in the interim report

This note is most relevant to **Section 1.5 Predecessor review: T-PLAT**. Its role is to help the report say two things at once:
