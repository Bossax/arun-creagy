# TOR 5.2.5 – Data Management Structure Note (Sources → CDM → NCAIF → Products)

This note summarises the **data management structure** underpinning NCAIF, as required by TOR 5.2.5 and v1 main report: Ch.1 §1.10, using the governance strategy in [`2026-03-05-Feature-Driven Data Governance Strategy v3.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-03-05-Feature-Driven%20Data%20Governance%20Strategy%20v3.md), the CDM design in [`2026-02-26-Detailed_CDM_Design.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-02-26-Detailed_CDM_Design.md), the NCAIF structure note, and the v1 appendices (Appendix ก – CDM conceptual model; Appendix ข – draft NCAIF structure).

## 1. Purpose and scope of the data management structure

TOR 5.2.5 asks the project to describe how climate-risk and adaptation data will be **managed and organised**, not only how they will be presented. In the interim report, this is expressed as a structure that connects:

1. **Source systems and datasets** (within DCCE and partner agencies);
2. the **Conceptual Data Model (CDM)** as the organising logic;
3. the **NCAIF information framework** as the user-facing structure; and
4. **products and services** (briefing packs, profiles, registries, etc.) that implement TOR tasks.

The data management structure is therefore both an **information architecture** and a **governance and process architecture**.

## 2. Source layer – where data originate

The project recognises that DCCE is **not the only producer** of relevant data. The source layer includes:

- **Hazard and climate datasets** from technical agencies (e.g. national meteorological and hydrological services, space and geospatial agencies) and research partners.
- **Socio-economic and demographic baselines** from the national statistics system and sectoral ministries.
- **Disaster and loss records** from disaster-management authorities and related systems.
- **Adaptation plans, measures, and project information** from DCCE and other ministries.

These sources may live in different platforms and registries. The data-management structure does not attempt to centralise all raw data into a single database in Phase 1. Instead, it focuses on **cataloguing, endorsing baselines, and defining how NCAIF products reference these sources**.

## 3. Conceptual Data Model (CDM) – organising logic

The CDM, documented in detail in the CDM design note and Appendix ก, provides the **semantic backbone** for how entities relate across the system. It defines subject areas such as:

- climate drivers and conditions;
- hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and risk outputs;
- loss and damage records;
- adaptation projects and measures; and
- resilience and adaptation results.

Each entity in the CDM carries fields for identifiers, relationships, spatial and temporal dimensions, and provenance. The CDM does **not** prescribe a specific physical database technology, but it constrains how data must be structured so that NCAIF pages and products can connect climate, risk, loss, and adaptation information consistently.

## 4. Governance rails and gates – how data move and are controlled

The governance strategy v3 defines three **publishing rails**:

- **Open data (Rail A)** – datasets that can be published openly via agency catalogs and harvested to the national open-data portal.
- **G2G / non-open data (Rail B)** – datasets that move between agencies via government data-exchange infrastructure.
- **Internal-only data (Rail C)** – datasets that remain internal to DCCE until governance and legal conditions allow broader sharing.

For each dataset or product that feeds into NCAIF, Phase 1 governance requires decisions and metadata on:

- **classification** (Internal / G2G / Open);
- **intended and prohibited uses**;
- **owner and steward roles**;
- **update cadence**; and

five minimum **governance gates (G1–G5)**:

1. **G1 – Data classification and publishing path** (which rail and why).
2. **G2 – Catalog metadata and preview standard** (minimum fields and preview expectations).
3. **G3 – Recommended baseline endorsement** (who can endorse, for what, and with what caveats).
4. **G4 – Boundary and crosswalk governance** (canonical boundaries for reporting and owned crosswalks for others).
5. **G5 – Event/impact schema governance** (timeliness, validation flags, and revision history for event and loss data).

These gates ensure that by the time data reach NCAIF as recommended baselines, profiles, or packs, they carry enough context to be used safely and reused.

## 5. NCAIF and catalog layer – how data are exposed and discovered

The NCAIF structure (see Section 5.2.3 artifacts) defines top-level sections such as the Policy Maker Centre, Adaptation Information by Cycle, Risk and Area Profiles, and Knowledge/Tools/Data Services. Underneath these sections, the data-management structure provides:

- a **catalog** of datasets and products, tagged by subject area, geography, sector, and rail;
- a **recommended baseline registry** for high-demand variables (MVP-3);
- **profiles and packs** that combine data, narrative, and caveats for different users.

Operationally, this means:

- The catalog exposes datasets with metadata (owner, source, cadence, limitations, access path) but does not necessarily host all data itself.
- The baseline registry records the **endorsed dataset for a given purpose** (e.g. provincial flood baseline), along with limitations and version history.
- NCAIF pages and packs draw on entries from the catalog and registry to assemble user-facing products.

This layer is where the **catalog-first, link-first** stance from the governance strategy is implemented: NCAIF can provide meaningful services even when data remain in agency systems, as long as metadata, classification, and endorsement are managed.

## 6. Product layer – how users experience the structure

The **product layer** consists of the outputs that users directly see, including:

- **Briefing packs and policy-maker summaries** (MVP-1 in the broader architecture, used here as a conceptual driver).
- **Risk and area profiles**, built around the spatial risk map but wrapped with narrative context and limitations.
- **Loss and damage views**, including post-event summaries and comparisons of relief payments vs broader economic loss (MVP-2 groundwork).
- **Use-case-oriented narratives and explainers**, such as cascading-impact explainers and vulnerable-group views.

Each product is traceable back to:

- one or more **recommended baselines** in the registry;
- CDM entities (e.g. `RISK_ASSESSMENT`, `LOSS_DAMAGE_RECORD`, `ADAPTATION_PROJECT`);
- datasets in the catalog (with owners and rails);
- governance gates passed (classification, QA/QC, endorsement).

This traceability is what allows NCAIF to act as a **clearinghouse** and to support later audit and revision.

## 7. Relationship to TOR 5.2.5 and the interim report

In the interim report, v1 Ch.1 §1.10 summarises this structure as a **draft data-management architecture** that:

- uses the CDM as the central organising logic;
- relies on national data infrastructure for hosting and exchange;
- introduces governance gates and rails tailored to Phase 1 MVPs; and
- positions NCAIF as a catalog- and registry-driven information framework.

The artifacts created on 2026‑03‑25 (revision log, this note, and the accompanying data-management table) provide the evidence needed to describe this architecture without re-deriving it during writing.
