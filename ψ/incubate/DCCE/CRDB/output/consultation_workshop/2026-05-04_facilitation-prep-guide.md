# Workshop Facilitation Guide: Reviewing the NCAIF Website Structure (Afternoon Session)
**Target Date**: 2026-05-12
**Goal**: Capture user expectations and needs for the **NCAIF website** (structure + content), using the workshop sitemap as the anchor. This session is not a governance forum; it is a structured requirements and expectations capture.

## Expected outputs (what you should walk away with)

1) **Top user tasks** the website must support (what people must be able to do quickly)
2) **Top missing pages/content** from the user perspective
3) **A prioritized needs log** (problem → expectation → required page/content → priority → notes)
4) **A wording/clarity list** (terms that are confusing + suggested simpler labels)

---

## Session stance (important)

- Treat this as **Validate + Guided Feedback**: present the prepared structure, then ask participants to correct, prioritize, and add missing needs.
- Do **not** attempt to resolve governance/decision-rights in the room. Capture expectations that DCCE can translate into internal decisions later.

---

## 1. Presentation Slides: "What this website is for" (10-15 Minutes)

**Slide 1: NCAIF — A national adaptation information website (front door)**
*   **Visual**: A simple map showing NCAIF as the center for data from all agencies (TMD, GISTDA, etc.).
*   **Narrative**: "NCAIF is a front door for adaptation information. It helps you find the right pages, the right evidence, and the right way to use it in planning and reporting."

**Slide 2: The national situation story (3 lenses)**
*   **Visual**: Three icons: 1. Past Damage, 2. Future Risk, 3. Our Progress.
*   **Narrative**: "We have designed a 3-step report to help you track the national situation:
    1. **What happened?** (Past disaster costs).
    2. **What is coming?** (Future risks).
    3. **What are we doing?** (How we are making progress)."

*   **Facilitator note**: This slide is a story for the website; ask participants which lens should be most prominent for their work.

**Slide 3: Resources that make the website usable (laws, funding, templates)**
*   **Visual**: Icons for the Climate Change Act and Funding Sources (GCF, Thai Environment Fund).
*   **Narrative**: "We provide a library of the **Climate Laws** and a list of **Funding Sources** to help you act on the data and pay for your projects."

**Slide 4: Step-by-step navigation (how the website should guide you)**
*   **Visual**: A flow chart: Science → Risks → Damage → Planning → Tracking.
*   **Narrative**: "This section follows the way you work. It starts with the **Science**, helps you assess the **Impact**, and ends with choosing the right **Solutions** (like planting trees or building walls)."

**Slide 5: Special tools and services (4 engines / MVPs — recap only)**
*   **Visual**: 4 simple icons for the specialized tools DCCE is building.
*   **Narrative**: "DCCE is building 4 special tools to power this website—including a data catalog and a tool to help you interpret risk maps."

**Slide 6: How we will capture your expectations**
*   **Visual**: A picture of the poster and sticky notes.
*   **Narrative**: "Now, we will ask for your opinion using your phones (Mentimeter) and by putting sticky notes on the posters around the room."

## 2. Mentimeter Questions: Structured expectation capture

### Question 1: What should users see first on province pages?
*   **Question**: "On a province profile page, what should be the first headline message?"
*   **Options**:
    1. Risk determinants (exposure/vulnerability/adaptive capacity)
    2. Impacts and Loss & Damage (observed costs/impacts)
    3. Both (but with a clear ordering + disclaimers)

### Question 2: What is your most common job-to-do on the website?
*   **Question**: "Which task do you need the website to support most often?"
*   **Options**:
    1. Quickly find a page to reuse in a report/briefing
    2. Find the official dataset/source and how to use it safely
    3. Compare areas/sectors at a high level (national/provincial)
    4. Export a figure/table/map for internal slides

### Question 3: What makes a page trustworthy enough to reuse?
*   **Question**: "Before you reuse information from this website, what must be visible on the page?"
*   **Options**:
    1. Last updated / version
    2. Data source agency
    3. Limitations / how to interpret safely
    4. Contact point / help channel

### Question 4: What is missing?
*   **Question**: "What is missing from the website structure for your work?"
*   **Format**: Word cloud (short phrases)

## 3. Poster Activities: Guided feedback on the website structure
*Place posters around the room. At minimum: 1 sitemap poster + 3–5 expectation posters.*

### Activity A: "Where is your work?" (keep)
*   **Instruction**: "Write one **job-to-do** (report / decision / workflow) you do regularly. Place it on the sitemap where you expect to find help."
*   **Goal**: Identify which sitemap nodes are critical vs unused.

### Activity B: "Missing content/pages" (new)
*   **Instruction**: "On the sitemap poster, add sticky notes for: (1) missing pages, (2) missing content blocks that must exist on a page (e.g., last updated, limitations)."
*   **Goal**: Capture concrete additions to the website structure and page templates.

### Activity C: "Is the language clear?" (keep, but make it actionable)
*   **Instruction**: "Use a **Red Sticker** on any word or category that is confusing or uses too much 'Consultant Jargon'."
*   **Add**: On a sticky note, propose a simpler replacement word.
*   **Goal**: Build a list of wording fixes for navigation + a glossary backlog.
