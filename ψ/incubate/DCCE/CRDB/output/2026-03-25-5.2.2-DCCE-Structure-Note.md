# TOR 5.2.2 – DCCE Current Climate-Risk Information Structure and Governance

This note summarizes the **current-state** structure of DCCE’s climate-risk information and governance, as required for Section 1 (subsections 1.2, 1.6–1.7) of the interim report. It is strictly descriptive and does **not** propose future design.

## 1. Overall role of DCCE in climate-risk information

The v1 interim report interprets the TOR as asking DCCE to develop a **national information framework and management structure**, not just a website. Within that framing, DCCE currently plays three overlapping roles:

1. **Producer and sponsor of climate/adaptation information products** – DCCE generates or commissions reports, risk assessments, and adaptation-related knowledge products (such as vulnerability studies and thematic reports).
2. **Operator or co-operator of key technical systems** – notably the **spatial risk-map platform** and related web-based tools in the DCCE web estate.
3. **Prospective coordinator for cross-agency climate information** – the department is expected to help align information from multiple ministries and agencies, even when it does not directly own all underlying datasets.

These roles are visible in the current web landscape and in the way DCCE has organized the CRDB project: it must both maintain existing assets and prepare to host the future NCAIF information framework inside the broader DCCE website.

## 2. Current content and system structure ("as-is")

### 2.1 Website as administration-first, portal-of-portals

The website gap summary describes the current DCCE website as **administration‑first and portal‑of‑portals**. The site emphasizes organizational structure and program labels rather than user tasks or adaptation cycles. Key characteristics include:

- Extensive **policy documents and national reports** presented as downloadable PDFs.
- Multiple **specialized technical systems** exposed via links or subdomains, including the climate change information center (CCIC), the spatial risk map, and SCP-related portals.
- Navigation organized primarily around **programs, projects, and internal divisions**, not around climate-risk questions or user journeys.

This structure reflects how information has historically been produced and published within DCCE: as project- or program-specific outputs, each with its own page or microsite.

### 2.2 Spatial risk map as flagship product

Ch.1 §1.6 of the v1 report highlights the **spatial risk-map system** as a flagship climate-risk product. In its current form:

- It offers **map-based views of relative risk indices** for selected hazards and administrative areas.
- It has become a central reference for discussions about provincial or area-level climate risk.
- It is integrated into the DCCE web estate as one significant but **standalone** product.

At the same time, the report notes important **limitations**: the risk indices are relative rather than absolute, spatial resolution is constrained, and interpretation requires care. These constraints underscore that the current risk map, while valuable, is not yet a complete national risk information backbone.

### 2.3 Fragmented knowledge and tools

Across the DCCE web estate, climate-related **knowledge, tools, and data services** are present but **fragmented**:

- Case studies, adaptation measures, and good practices appear in **news items or project pages**, without a unified library.
- Tools and data services (for example CCIC, data catalog functions, or SCP portals) live on **separate subdomains or pages**, each with its own navigation and branding.
- Methods, standards, and caveats are frequently embedded inside PDF reports rather than exposed as dedicated guidance pages.

The gap summary concludes that users must **manually piece together** information from multiple systems and documents to follow the climate → risk → adaptation story.

## 3. Current governance and responsibility pattern

### 3.1 Internal roles (owner, steward, custodian)

The CRDB plan and interview-preparation notes distinguish between **data owner**, **data steward**, and **system custodian** roles. In current practice:

- For products and knowledge generated directly by DCCE (e.g. certain risk assessments or adaptation reports), DCCE acts as the **data owner** and **publisher**.
- For shared or external datasets, DCCE often functions as a **user and convenor** rather than the primary owner; datasets may originate from agencies such as DDPM, DGA, NSO, or sectoral ministries.
- Operational responsibility for web infrastructure and some systems is shared with **central digital or IT teams**, meaning DCCE is not the sole technical custodian even when it is the thematic owner.

This division of roles is already visible in how current systems are run: climate-risk content and analysis are led by DCCE, while certain data pipelines and hosting arrangements rely on cross-government infrastructure.

### 3.2 Publishing rails and external platforms

The plan document summarizes an emerging **publishing-rails stance** that reflects current practice and constraints:

- **Open data** are expected to flow through **data.go.th**.
- **Government-to-government exchanges** are expected to use the **GDX** infrastructure.
- **Sensitive or internal data** remain on internal systems.

Even before the NCAIF architecture is fully implemented, this pattern shapes how DCCE can practically share data derived from its climate-risk work. It also explains why several current systems operate more as **front-ends** that link to or visualize data residing in other government platforms.

## 4. How the current structure affects NCAIF design

From the evidence above, the v1 report draws several implications that must be acknowledged in Section 1.6–1.7:

1. **Real assets, but fragmented organization.** DCCE already hosts meaningful climate and adaptation assets—reports, maps, tools—but they are distributed across programs and subdomains. NCAIF cannot assume a blank slate; it must build on and reorganize these assets.

2. **Website as host, not replacement.** The interim report emphasizes that NCAIF will be **hosted inside the existing DCCE website**, not positioned as a completely separate site that replaces current systems. The current portal-of-portals structure is therefore a starting condition for any new information framework.

3. **Need for user- and journey-based navigation.** The current administration-first navigation means that policy makers, technical analysts, and local implementers lack a clear path to decision-ready content. This gap motivates the use of **user journeys and adaptation-cycle logic** as organizing principles in the future NCAIF sitemap.

4. **Governance gaps around metadata and responsibility.** Today, metadata standards, update expectations, and cross-agency governance are only partially explicit. The project’s later emphasis on CDM and governance gates arises directly from observing that current DCCE information products do not yet sit within a fully defined management structure.

For Section 1 of the interim report, this note allows the text to **describe the current DCCE landscape factually**—what units and systems exist, how they relate, and where fragmentation lies—without drifting into future-state design. Section 1.6 can then focus on analytical implications, while Section 1.7 links current user-experience evidence to the need for NCAIF.
