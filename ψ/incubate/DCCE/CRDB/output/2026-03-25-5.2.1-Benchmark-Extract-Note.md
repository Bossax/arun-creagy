# TOR 5.2.1 – Benchmark Platforms (Section 1.5 Evidence Note)
## 1. Role of the benchmark platforms in the CRDB project

TOR 5.2.1 requires the project to study benchmark climate adaptation platforms such as **A-PLAT (Japan)**, **KlimAdapt/KLiVO Portal (Germany)**, and **Climate-ADAPT (European Union)**. In the inception report and the first interim report, these platforms are not treated as generic international examples; they are used to clarify **what kind of information service NCAIF must become**.

The reading consolidated in [`ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-03-13-Three-Platform Review and T-PLAT Lessons.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-03-13-Three-Platform%20Review%20and%20T-PLAT%20Lessons.md) and summarized in **v1 main report: Ch.1 §1.5** leads to three shared lessons:

1. A national adaptation platform is **not** just a static document library.
2. A credible platform needs **multiple coordinated layers**: policy guidance, tools/services, knowledge and case material, and explicit curation/governance.
3. The platform must manage **scale and diversity of users**, not assume a single generic audience.

These lessons shape Section 1.5 of the interim report: the subsection explains that NCAIF is being designed as a **service-oriented information framework** that inherits what works from the benchmark platforms while adapting to the Thai context and the TOR’s emphasis on data management and governance.

## 2. A-PLAT (Japan)

**Purpose and user focus.** A-PLAT functions as a **national adaptation information platform** with clear pathways for central government, local government, the private sector, and citizens. It combines policy and planning information with service layers such as climate and impact atlases, sector pages, and local adaptation resources.

**Key features.** From the project’s perspective, A-PLAT’s strongest contribution is **translation**: it makes technical climate and impact knowledge legible for different user groups without collapsing them into a single audience. The platform offers:

- sector- and stakeholder-specific entry points;
- integrated maps and atlases that link climate indicators to impacts; and
- curated adaptation examples tied back to national policy objectives.

**Limitations and relevance.** A-PLAT is still largely organized as a **national portal**. It demonstrates how to present complex climate information clearly, but it does not on its own provide a blueprint for a fully-fledged national data governance system. For NCAIF, the lesson is that **user-specific pathways and explanatory content are necessary but not sufficient**; they must sit on top of a conceptual data model and governance structure that can sustain integration across multiple Thai agencies.

## 3. KlimAdapt / KLiVO Portal (Germany)

**Purpose and user focus.** KlimAdapt/KLiVO operates less as a single content website and more as a **curated access layer to climate-preparedness services**. Users arrive with specific planning tasks and are routed toward relevant tools, datasets, and guidance.

**Key features.** Its defining move is the **service catalogue**. Users can filter by planning purpose, field of action, climate variable, climate impact, region, target group, and category. Behind this catalogue is explicit metadata discipline and inclusion criteria.

**Limitations and relevance.** KlimAdapt/KLiVO does not attempt to centralize all content; instead, it orchestrates access to distributed services. For NCAIF, this illustrates that **curation and quality control are core functions**: the framework must include a catalogue-like layer that tags and routes products from DCCE and partner agencies, with clear rules about what qualifies as a reference asset. At the same time, NCAIF cannot rely only on a thin routing layer; it also needs internal structure (CDM, NCAIF subject areas) to support Thai-specific integration and governance.

## 4. Climate-ADAPT (European Union)

**Purpose and user focus.** Climate-ADAPT functions as a **multi-level knowledge hub** that links EU adaptation policy, national and local profiles, data explorers, tools, indicators, case studies, and resource catalogues. It addresses policymakers, practitioners, and analysts who need a cross-scale picture of risks and adaptation responses.

**Key features.** Its strongest contribution for the CRDB project is **scale management**:

- connections between EU-level strategies and country profiles;
- data explorers and indicators that can be reused across geographies; and
- case-study and knowledge catalogues that can be filtered by sector, hazard, and theme.

Climate-ADAPT shows that a platform can **separate** policy guidance pages, searchable resource catalogues, and specialist tools while keeping them linked through a shared taxonomy and metadata schema.

**Limitations and relevance.** Climate-ADAPT is optimized for the EU governance context. Direct replication is not possible for NCAIF, but the **clearinghouse model**—where a central platform links authoritative external sources rather than owning all data—aligns with stakeholder expectations in Thailand and with DCCE’s mandate. For NCAIF, the implication is that it should act as a **national adaptation clearinghouse**: a place where users can reliably find and interpret climate-risk information across agencies, even when the underlying data remain in distributed systems.

## 5. Cross-cutting lessons for NCAIF (as expressed in Section 1.5)

When read together, the three platforms establish a **minimum set of architectural expectations** for NCAIF:

1. **Four-layer service structure.** Section 1.5 should make explicit that NCAIF is being designed with at least four layers working together:
   - a **policy and guidance layer** for mandates, strategies, and explanatory framing;
   - a **service and tool layer** for maps, assessment tools, dashboards, and other interactive functions;
   - a **knowledge and case layer** for reports, case studies, and learning materials; and
   - a **curation and governance layer** for metadata, quality control, update responsibility, and user-group pathways.

2. **Beyond static portals.** All three platforms confirm that a modern adaptation platform must go beyond static web pages and PDF lists. For NCAIF, this supports the argument that the project is building a **national information architecture**, not only a new website.

3. **User- and task-oriented navigation.** A-PLAT and KlimAdapt/KLiVO reinforce that entry points should follow user tasks (policy design, local planning, technical analysis) rather than internal organizational charts. Section 1.5 can rely on this evidence when explaining why the NCAIF sitemap uses policy-maker, sector, place, and workflow entry points instead of mirroring DCCE’s organogram.

4. **Clearinghouse and interoperability logic.** Climate-ADAPT demonstrates how a central hub can connect distributed datasets, tools, and case libraries. This underpins the NCAIF design choice to focus on **interoperability, cataloguing, and governance rules** rather than rebuilding all underlying systems inside a single platform.

5. **Predecessor and Thai context (via T-PLAT).** The v1 report and the dedicated benchmark note treat **T-PLAT** as the key Thai predecessor influenced by these platforms. The lesson carried into Section 1.5 is that NCAIF must keep T-PLAT’s strengths (visible services, sector coverage, tested audience logic) but address its structural weaknesses (portal-only design, limited metadata and governance). This justifies NCAIF’s emphasis on CDM, data governance, and a long-lived national architecture.

Taken together, these points satisfy TOR 5.2.1 for the interim report: Section 1.5 can draw directly from this note to explain **which platforms were reviewed, what they showed, and how their lessons were concretely applied** in the current NCAIF design, without re-opening a full literature survey during drafting.
