# 1. Executive Summary

This report presents the findings of the Provincial Capacity-Based Index
(CBI) assessment conducted across all 77 provinces of Thailand as part
of the Climate Risk Index Phase 2 (CRI Phase 2) project. The CBI
measures the capacity of each province to cope with, adapt to, and
transform in response to climate-related risks, using 22 indicators
across three pillars: Coping, Adaptive, and Transformative capacity.

**Key Findings**

- Only 1 province (Bangkok) is rated \"High\" capacity with a CBI score
  of 84.0, reflecting its concentration of national resources,
  infrastructure, and institutional capacity.

- 7 provinces (9%) are rated \"Moderate\" capacity: Chon Buri (66.3),
  Rayong (58.7), Phra Nakhon Si Ayutthaya (58.6), Samut Prakan (58.4),
  Prachin Buri (58.1), Phuket (54.4), and Nonthaburi (53.0).

- 37 provinces (48%) are rated \"Low\" capacity, and 32 provinces (42%)
  are rated \"Very Low\" --- meaning 90% of Thailand\'s provinces fall
  below the Moderate threshold.

- The national average CBI score is 30.4 out of 100, placing the country
  as a whole in the \"Low\" capacity category.

- Significant regional disparity exists: the East region records the
  highest average CBI (47.9), driven by Eastern Economic Corridor (EEC)
  investments, while the Northeast region records the lowest (17.5).

- Pillar averages reveal broadly consistent weakness: Coping (31.4),
  Adaptive (30.4), and Transformative (29.4), with Transformative
  capacity being the weakest.

- Critical gaps persist in Northeast Thailand, where all 20 provinces
  scored below the national average, highlighting systemic
  underdevelopment in climate resilience infrastructure.

These findings underscore the urgent need for targeted investment in
provincial climate resilience, with particular focus on the Northeast
region and the 32 \"Very Low\" capacity provinces. The following
sections detail the methodology, results, and policy recommendations for
addressing these critical gaps.

# 2. Introduction & Background

Thailand faces significant and growing climate risks. According to the
ND-GAIN Country Index, Thailand ranks 70th globally with a vulnerability
score of 0.435 and a readiness score of 0.489[^1]. These figures
indicate that while Thailand has made progress in building adaptive
capacity at the national level, substantial gaps remain --- particularly
at the provincial and local levels, where climate impacts are most
directly experienced.

The Capacity-Based Index (CBI) was developed as a core component of the
Climate Risk Index Phase 2 (CRI Phase 2) project, commissioned by the
Department of Climate Change and Environment (DCCE) and implemented by
Creagy Company Limited. While CRI Phase 1 focused on hazard exposure and
vulnerability assessment, Phase 2 expands the framework to incorporate a
comprehensive measure of provincial-level capacity to respond to, adapt
to, and transform in the face of climate change.

Thailand submitted its National Adaptation Plan (NAP) to the UNFCCC in
April 2024[^2], identifying six priority sectors for adaptation: water
resource management, agriculture and food security, tourism, public
health, natural resource management, and human settlement and security.
The NAP provides a national-level framework, but effective
implementation requires understanding capacity at the provincial level
--- the primary unit of subnational governance in Thailand.

The DCCE, under the Ministry of Natural Resources and Environment, holds
the mandate for coordinating national climate change policy and
monitoring. Recognizing that climate impacts vary dramatically across
Thailand\'s 77 provinces --- from flood-prone central plains to
drought-affected northeastern plateaus to coastal southern regions
exposed to storm surges --- the DCCE commissioned this provincial-level
capacity assessment to inform targeted investment and policy
interventions.

The urgency of this assessment is underscored by recent disaster events.
In 2024, severe flooding affected 47 provinces across Thailand,
impacting approximately 256,400 households and causing 57
fatalities[^3]. The scale and distribution of these impacts highlight
the critical need for understanding and strengthening provincial-level
capacity to manage climate-related disasters.

This assessment directly supports Thailand\'s Nationally Determined
Contribution (NDC) commitments and its Long-Term Strategy, which targets
carbon neutrality by 2050 and net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2065.
By providing a granular, province-by-province capacity baseline, the CBI
enables evidence-based allocation of adaptation resources and tracking
of progress over time.

The CBI is designed to be complementary to the vulnerability and hazard
components of the CRI framework. Together, these indices provide a
comprehensive picture of climate risk: where hazards are greatest, where
populations are most vulnerable, and --- critically --- where the
capacity to respond is weakest. This integrated approach allows
policymakers to identify provinces where the combination of high
exposure, high vulnerability, and low capacity creates the most urgent
need for intervention.

# 3. Methodology

## 3.1 Conceptual Framework

The CBI is built upon the CRI Phase 2 methodology developed jointly by
DCCE and Creagy Company Limited. The conceptual framework draws on
established climate resilience theory, which distinguishes three
distinct but interrelated types of capacity:

- **Coping Capacity** --- The ability to respond to and manage immediate
  climate-related disasters and emergencies, including emergency
  response systems, disaster funds, rescue capabilities, and medical
  personnel.

- **Adaptive Capacity** --- The ability to adjust systems and practices
  to reduce vulnerability to climate impacts over the medium term,
  including early warning systems, insurance mechanisms, community
  participation, and climate literacy.

- **Transformative Capacity** --- The ability to fundamentally
  restructure systems and institutions to build long-term climate
  resilience, including green infrastructure, climate-resilient
  development, innovation adoption, and systemic planning.

Each pillar is further divided into two dimensions:

- **Asset Dimension** --- Physical, financial, and human resources
  available for climate response (tangible inputs).

- **Process Dimension** --- Institutional mechanisms, planning
  processes, and governance structures that determine how effectively
  assets are deployed.

This 3-pillar × 2-dimension structure yields 6 sub-dimensions, across
which 22 indicators are distributed. The framework ensures comprehensive
coverage of both the \"what\" (resources) and \"how\" (governance) of
climate capacity.

## 3.3 Normalization & Scoring

All indicators are normalized to a common 0--1 scale using Min-Max
normalization to enable cross-indicator comparison and aggregation:

**For positive indicators (higher = better capacity):**

*Normalized Value = (X − Min) / (Max − Min)*

**For negative indicators (lower = better capacity, e.g., CP3 Warning
Response Time):**

*Normalized Value = (Max − X) / (Max − Min)*

The aggregation follows an equal-weighting scheme:

- Each of the three pillars (Coping, Adaptive, Transformative) receives
  equal weight: 33.33%.

- Within each pillar, the Asset and Process dimensions are weighted
  equally: 50/50.

- Within each dimension, individual indicators are weighted equally.

- The final CBI score is computed as the weighted sum of all normalized
  indicator values, scaled to a 0--100 range.

Provinces are classified into four capacity levels based on their final
CBI score:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
         **Level**            **Score Range**       **Interpretation**
  ----------------------- ----------------------- -----------------------
         Very Low                  \< 25               Critical gaps

            Low                  25 -- 50             Below adequate

         Moderate                50 -- 75            Adequate baseline

           High                    ≥ 75               Strong capacity

## 3.4 Data Sources

The CBI assessment draws on multiple official data sources to ensure
credibility and replicability:

- Ministry of Public Health (MOPH) personnel statistics for fiscal year
  2567 --- used for indicator CA4 (Emergency Medical Personnel per
  10,000 population).[^5]

- Gross Provincial Product (GPP) per capita data for 2019, published by
  the National Economic and Social Development Council (NESDC) --- used
  as an economic capacity proxy.[^6]

- Provincial population data as of December 2024, from the Department of
  Provincial Administration (DOPA) --- used for all per-capita
  calculations.[^7]

- Department of Disaster Prevention and Mitigation (DDPM) disaster
  statistics for 2024 --- providing context on flood impacts across
  provinces.[^8]

- Local Performance Assessment (LPA) data from the Department of Local
  Administration --- used for governance and process indicators.

- National Statistical Office (NSO) provincial statistics --- used for
  socioeconomic and infrastructure indicators.

- Province-level government capacity assessments conducted during CRI
  Phase 2 field surveys.

# 4. Results & Analysis

## 4.1 National Overview

The national average CBI score across all 77 provinces is 30.4, placing
Thailand as a whole in the \"Low\" capacity category. This average masks
enormous variation: scores range from 11.0 (Nakhon Phanom) to 84.0
(Bangkok), a gap of 73 points on a 100-point scale.

The distribution of provinces across capacity levels reveals a
concerning picture:

- High (≥ 75): 1 province (1%) --- Bangkok only

- Moderate (50--75): 7 provinces (9%) --- concentrated in the Eastern
  Seaboard and Bangkok Metropolitan Region

- Low (25--50): 37 provinces (48%) --- the largest group, spanning all
  regions

- Very Low (\< 25): 32 provinces (42%) --- predominantly in the
  Northeast and Deep South

With 90% of provinces falling below the Moderate threshold, the results
indicate a systemic deficit in climate resilience capacity at the
subnational level. Bangkok is a clear outlier, benefiting from its
status as the capital city with concentrated national institutions,
financial resources, and infrastructure.

The East region leads with an average CBI of 47.9, primarily due to
sustained investment under the Eastern Economic Corridor (EEC)
initiative. Provinces such as Chon Buri, Rayong, and Chachoengsao have
benefited from industrial development, infrastructure modernization, and
enhanced government services associated with EEC development[^9].

The Central region\'s average of 40.7 is significantly influenced by
Bangkok (84.0) and its adjacent provinces (Nonthaburi, Samut Prakan,
Nakhon Pathom, Pathum Thani). Excluding the Bangkok Metropolitan Region,
the Central region\'s average would fall considerably, indicating that
capacity is concentrated in the urban core rather than distributed
across the region.

The Northeast region records the lowest average CBI at 17.5, with all 20
provinces scoring below the national average. This region faces
compounding challenges: lower economic development, predominantly
agricultural livelihoods vulnerable to climate variability, limited
industrial base, and lower levels of government investment in resilience
infrastructure. Khon Kaen, the region\'s largest urban center, leads
with 24.6 --- still classified as \"Very Low.\"

The South region exhibits the widest intra-regional variation, ranging
from Phuket (54.4) to Narathiwat (13.4). Phuket\'s tourism-driven
economy supports greater capacity, while the three southernmost
provinces (Narathiwat, Yala, Pattani) face ongoing conflict that
disrupts governance and development, compounding climate vulnerability.

## 4.3 Pillar Analysis

The three capacity pillars show broadly similar national averages, with
Coping slightly higher and Transformative slightly lower:

- Coping Capacity (national average: 31.4): Emergency response
  infrastructure and personnel are strongest in Bangkok and major
  industrial provinces. Bangkok\'s coping score benefits from
  national-level emergency management institutions, extensive medical
  facilities, and well-equipped rescue services. Rural provinces,
  particularly in the Northeast, often rely on volunteer-based disaster
  response with limited equipment and training.

- Adaptive Capacity (national average: 30.4): This pillar reveals
  critical gaps in disaster insurance coverage, which averages below 15%
  in most Northeastern provinces. Early warning systems are concentrated
  in historically flood-prone zones, particularly along the Chao Phraya
  basin and eastern coastal areas. Community participation in climate
  planning varies widely, with higher engagement in provinces with
  active civil society organizations.

- Transformative Capacity (national average: 29.4): This is the weakest
  pillar overall, reflecting the nascent state of systemic climate
  transformation in Thai governance. Climate innovation projects are
  concentrated in fewer than 10 provinces, predominantly those hosting
  major universities (Bangkok, Chiang Mai, Khon Kaen, Songkhla).
  Climate-informed zoning remains rare, and economic transition planning
  has not yet been adopted in most provinces.

## 4.5 Key Gaps Identified

The assessment reveals several critical gaps in Thailand\'s provincial
climate capacity:

- 90% of provinces (69 of 77) fall below the Moderate capacity
  threshold, indicating that the vast majority of Thailand\'s
  subnational units lack adequate climate resilience capacity.

- Disaster insurance penetration averages less than 15% in Northeastern
  provinces, leaving rural populations financially exposed to climate
  shocks --- particularly significant given the region\'s dependence on
  rain-fed agriculture.

- Only 1 province (Bangkok) qualifies as \"High\" capacity, and only 7
  additional provinces reach \"Moderate,\" demonstrating that strong
  capacity is the exception rather than the norm.

- Climate adaptation plans are absent in approximately 40% of provinces,
  meaning that a substantial portion of the country lacks formal
  planning frameworks for climate adaptation.

- Climate innovation activities (TP3) are concentrated in fewer than 10
  provinces, all of which host major universities or research
  institutions, revealing a significant urban-rural innovation divide.

- The gap between the highest-scoring region (East: 47.9) and the lowest
  (Northeast: 17.5) is 30.4 points --- equivalent to the entire national
  average --- demonstrating the scale of regional inequity.

# 5. Policy Recommendations

Based on the assessment findings, the following recommendations are
organized by priority level and time horizon to guide strategic
investment in provincial climate resilience capacity.

## 5.1 Priority Action Areas

1.  **Targeted Capacity Building for Northeast Thailand** *(Critical
    Priority)*

> Establish a dedicated funding program for the 20 Northeastern
> provinces, addressing the widest capacity gap (regional average 17.5
> versus national average 30.4). This program should prioritize
> emergency response infrastructure, disaster insurance expansion, and
> climate adaptation planning support. A phased approach --- beginning
> with the 10 lowest-scoring provinces --- would maximize impact.

2.  **National Disaster Insurance Expansion** *(High Priority)*

> Develop a government-subsidized crop and property insurance program
> specifically targeting rural provinces where current penetration is
> below 15%. This should build on existing programs such as the rice
> insurance scheme, expanding coverage to include flooding, drought, and
> other climate-related events. Public-private partnerships with
> insurance companies should be explored to ensure financial
> sustainability.

3.  **Decentralized Climate Adaptation Planning** *(High Priority)*

> Mandate and support the development of provincial Climate Adaptation
> Plans in all provinces. With approximately 40% of provinces still
> lacking formal adaptation plans, DCCE should provide technical
> assistance, template frameworks, and dedicated funding to close this
> gap within 2 years. Plans should be integrated with existing
> provincial development strategies.

4.  **Early Warning System Expansion** *(High Priority)*

> Scale proven early warning systems from flood-prone areas to all 77
> provinces. Currently, systems are concentrated along major river
> basins and coastal zones. Expansion should leverage mobile technology
> and community-based monitoring networks to extend coverage to remote
> areas, particularly in the North and Northeast regions.

5.  **Climate Innovation Hubs** *(Medium Priority)*

> Establish three regional Climate Innovation Centers in Khon Kaen
> (Northeast), Chiang Mai (North), and Songkhla (South), connected to
> existing university research infrastructure. These hubs would serve as
> catalysts for climate innovation diffusion to surrounding provinces,
> addressing the current concentration of innovation in fewer than 10
> provinces.

6.  **Strengthen Transformative Capacity** *(Medium Priority)*

> As the weakest pillar (average 29.4), transformative capacity requires
> dedicated attention. Key interventions include: promoting
> climate-informed zoning in provincial land-use plans, investing in
> green infrastructure and buffer zones, supporting local economic
> transition planning, and funding climate-resilient infrastructure
> upgrades in vulnerable areas.

7.  **Provincial CBI Monitoring System** *(Medium Priority)*

> Establish an annual CBI data collection and tracking mechanism within
> DCCE\'s existing monitoring framework. Integrate CBI indicators with
> the Local Performance Assessment (LPA) process to leverage existing
> provincial reporting infrastructure and create incentives for capacity
> improvement.

## 5.2 Quick Wins (Within 1 Year)

- Deploy mobile early warning applications in all 77 provinces,
  leveraging existing smartphone penetration to rapidly expand warning
  coverage at minimal cost.

- Conduct disaster preparedness drills in all 32 \"Very Low\" provinces,
  prioritizing the 10 lowest-scoring provinces for immediate
  implementation.

- Launch targeted climate literacy campaigns in the 32 \"Very Low\"
  capacity provinces, using local media, community leaders, and social
  media to raise awareness of climate risks and preparedness measures.

- Establish a provincial CBI baseline database to enable annual tracking
  and comparison.

## 5.3 Medium-Term Actions (1--3 Years)

- Establish a provincial Climate Adaptation Fund mechanism with
  dedicated budget allocation based on CBI scores --- provinces with
  lower scores should receive proportionally higher funding.

- Integrate CBI indicators into the existing Local Performance
  Assessment (LPA) framework, creating institutional incentives for
  provincial administrations to invest in climate resilience capacity.

- Develop province-specific climate vulnerability and capacity reports
  combining CRI hazard/vulnerability data with CBI capacity assessments,
  providing each province with a tailored action plan.

- Implement a capacity-building training program for provincial
  officials on climate adaptation planning, disaster risk management,
  and climate-informed decision-making.

## 5.4 Long-Term Vision (3--10 Years)

The long-term vision for Thailand\'s provincial climate capacity aligns
with national climate commitments and global frameworks:

- Target: All 77 provinces achieve \"Low\" capacity (CBI ≥ 25) or better
  by 2030, eliminating the \"Very Low\" category entirely.

- Target: At least 50% of provinces (39+) achieve \"Moderate\" capacity
  (CBI ≥ 50) or better by 2035, representing a fundamental shift in
  Thailand\'s provincial climate resilience.

- Align provincial capacity building with Thailand\'s Long-Term Strategy
  targets: carbon neutrality by 2050 and net-zero greenhouse gas
  emissions by 2065.

- Establish Thailand as a regional leader in subnational climate
  capacity assessment, sharing the CBI methodology with ASEAN partner
  countries facing similar challenges.

# 6. Limitations & Future Work

While the CBI provides a valuable and systematic assessment of
provincial climate capacity, several limitations should be acknowledged:

- Data availability: Some indicators rely on proxy or estimated data
  where official provincial-level statistics are unavailable. This is
  particularly true for newer indicators such as climate-resilient
  infrastructure (TA3) and climate innovation adoption (TP3), where
  standardized provincial data collection mechanisms do not yet exist.

- Binary indicators: Several indicators (CP1, AP1, TP1, TP2) use binary
  (0/1) scoring, which may oversimplify nuanced differences in capacity.
  A province with a rudimentary disaster response plan scores the same
  as one with a comprehensive, well-resourced plan. Future iterations
  should consider graduated scoring scales.

- Equal weighting: The current methodology applies equal weights across
  pillars, dimensions, and indicators. While this approach is
  transparent and avoids subjective weighting decisions, it may not
  fully reflect the relative importance of different capacity dimensions
  in specific contexts. Stakeholder consultation could inform
  context-sensitive weighting.

- Temporal snapshot: The CBI represents capacity at a single point in
  time. Climate capacity is dynamic and can change rapidly due to policy
  decisions, budget allocations, or disaster events. Regular
  reassessment is essential.

- Limited field validation: While the methodology draws on established
  frameworks and official data sources, field-level validation with
  provincial stakeholders has been limited. Ground-truthing of indicator
  values through provincial workshops would strengthen confidence in the
  results.

**Recommended Future Work**

Based on these limitations, the following areas are recommended for
future development:

- Conduct field validation workshops with provincial stakeholders in a
  representative sample of provinces across all regions and capacity
  levels to verify indicator values and gather qualitative insights.

- Integrate the CBI with CRI vulnerability and hazard data to produce
  composite climate risk profiles for each province, enabling holistic
  risk-based prioritization.

- Develop a dedicated data collection mechanism for CBI indicators
  within DCCE\'s monitoring framework, reducing reliance on proxy data
  and improving consistency over time.

- Explore differential weighting approaches through expert consultation
  and sensitivity analysis to understand how alternative weighting
  schemes affect provincial rankings and policy recommendations.

- Extend the assessment to sub-provincial (district/tambon) level in
  high-priority regions to enable more granular targeting of
  interventions.
