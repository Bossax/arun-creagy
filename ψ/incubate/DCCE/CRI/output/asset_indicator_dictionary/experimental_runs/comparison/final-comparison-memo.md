# CRI Phase 2 v4 Final Comparison Memo

## Status

Completed.

## Comparison basis

This memo synthesizes the completed final summaries for Experiment A, Experiment B, and Experiment C against the shared 1-to-5 scoring rubric approved in the execution plan.

## Executive comparison outcome

The preferred working default candidate is **Experiment B**.

The main reason is not that Experiment B radically changes the taxonomy. It does not. The main reason is that it preserves the six-pillar v3 kernel, reaches full coverage across all 11 hardened asset concepts, and improves the treatment of the two hardest community-mediated assets with only **one** tightly bounded overlay concept: **Community Self-Organization & Stewardship**.

Experiment A remains the strongest conservative baseline because it achieves full coverage with zero surviving concept growth. However, it does so by relying more heavily on combination chains for the softest cases, especially HC-09 and HC-11, which weakens bottleneck clarity and administrative readability relative to Experiment B.

Experiment C is best interpreted as a successful stress test rather than as the working default. Its final stabilized shape converges with Experiment B, but it reaches that outcome through looser Round 2 admission and very high Round 3 pruning. That makes it highly valuable as boundary evidence, but less suitable than Experiment B as the operational default for downstream drafting.

## 1. Final concept inventory delta versus v3

- **Experiment A:** `+0` surviving governance concepts. No new governance family and no thin overlay admitted.
- **Experiment B:** `+1` surviving thin overlay concept: `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship`.
- **Experiment C:** `+1` surviving thin overlay concept after exploratory expansion and Round 3 pruning: `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship`.

### Comparison finding

Across all three experiments, the program did **not** produce evidence for a broad seventh governance family. The only durable pressure point was a narrow community-mediated linkage problem affecting the two softest asset cases. This means the comparison outcome supports **bounded supplementation**, not taxonomy redesign.

## 2. Coverage summary across all 11 hardened asset concepts

All three experiments reached readable activation chains for all 11 hardened asset concepts and assigned one named primary bottleneck per chain.

The practical difference lies in **how** the hardest cases were stabilized:

- **Experiment A** covered HC-09 and HC-11 by combining existing kernel concepts only.
- **Experiment B** covered HC-09 and HC-11 with one bounded overlay, while keeping the other nine asset families inside interpretation-note or kernel-combination logic.
- **Experiment C** initially explored a wider admission path, then pruned back to the same final two-asset overlay pattern as Experiment B.

### Comparison finding

Coverage is no longer the discriminating criterion. The discriminating criteria are bottleneck clarity, category purity under pressure, evidence strength, and practitioner readability.

## 3. Surviving new concepts and why they survived

### Experiment A

- No surviving new concepts.

### Experiment B

- One surviving concept: **Community Self-Organization & Stewardship**.
- It survived because the evidence package supported a narrow claim: some asset stocks depend on informal stewardship, customary or local knowledge, trust-based self-organization, and autonomous collective action that are only partially legible through the six formal readiness categories alone.
- Its use was tightly bounded to **HC-09** and **HC-11**.

### Experiment C

- One surviving concept: **Community Self-Organization & Stewardship**.
- Its survival matters for a different reason than in Experiment B. Experiment C tested whether looser exploratory admission would justify more growth. After very high pruning, it did not. The final result retained only the same thin overlay that survived in Experiment B.

### Comparison finding

The shared signal from Experiments B and C is that only **one** narrow overlay survived, and it survived for only **two** asset families. This is strong evidence that the missing logic is limited and specific rather than systemic across the taxonomy.

## 4. Bottleneck clarity comparison

- **Experiment A:** Strong but not optimal. All chains are readable, but seven assets required combination-heavy linkage logic. This makes the primary bottleneck legible yet sometimes less immediate for a practitioner.
- **Experiment B:** Strongest. The retained overlay improves diagnostic precision for HC-09 and HC-11 without creating a new audit-heavy layer. This gives the clearest one-bottleneck story across the full set.
- **Experiment C:** Strong in final form, but the pathway is more complex. The final chains are clear, yet the experiment's own history shows that clarity was achieved after exploratory inflation followed by pruning.

### Comparison finding

Experiment B performs best on bottleneck clarity because it achieves the same full coverage as A and C while reducing ambiguity in the two cases where the kernel alone was least comfortable.

## 5. Category-purity issues by experiment

- **Experiment A:** Strongest category purity. Asset stock and governance activation remain fully separated, with no surviving overlay concept.
- **Experiment B:** Limited but manageable category-boundary strain around informal, customary, and community-mediated governance processes. The strain is contained by explicitly treating the new concept as a **thin linkage overlay**, not a new governance pillar.
- **Experiment C:** The same residual strain as Experiment B in the final stabilized result, plus additional evidence that looser exploratory settings can temporarily inflate that boundary if pruning is not enforced aggressively.

### Comparison finding

Experiment A is best on purity in the strict sense. However, Experiment B keeps purity within an acceptable range because the overlay remains narrow, non-pillar, and asset-limited. Experiment C confirms that this boundary must stay tightly governed.

## 6. Evidence-strength comparison

- **Experiment A:** Strong reuse-case evidence, but the two softest assets remained conservative judgments under a high bar for concept admission. Evidence was sufficient for rejection of growth, but less decisive for explaining the residual discomfort in HC-09 and HC-11.
- **Experiment B:** Strongest default-decision evidence. It combined the approved saved raw packets with a targeted gap check, a pruning review, and a successful web-grounded retry that converged on one bounded overlay rather than a broader taxonomy extension.
- **Experiment C:** Strong stress-test evidence. It adds value by showing that exploratory admission still converges to the same bounded result after very high pruning, even where one web-grounded source pushed toward two concepts.

### Comparison finding

Experiment B and Experiment C are equally strong on raw evidence score, but they answer different decision needs. Experiment B provides the best evidence for the **working default**. Experiment C provides the best evidence for the **outer boundary** of acceptable concept growth.

## 7. Cross-asset reusability and administrative readability

- **Experiment A:** Very high cross-asset reusability because it relies entirely on the existing kernel, but readability is somewhat reduced by combination-heavy explanations in the hardest cases.
- **Experiment B:** Best operational balance. The kernel still does most of the work across the portfolio, and the one overlay is reused across more than one asset type without distorting the broader taxonomy. This keeps the structure concise and decision-oriented.
- **Experiment C:** Final-form readability is comparable to Experiment B, but its exploratory lineage is less efficient as a working model because users could misread the Round 2 provisional growth as a standing design invitation rather than a stress-test phase.

### Comparison finding

Experiment B is the cleanest administrative package for downstream use because it captures the only durable gap signal without normalizing exploratory inflation.

## Recommendation

Select **Experiment B** as the preferred working default candidate for the approved CRI Phase 2 v4 linkage program.

### Why Experiment B is preferred

1. It preserves the six-pillar kernel rather than redesigning the taxonomy.
2. It reaches full 11-asset coverage with one primary bottleneck per chain.
3. It admits only one surviving overlay concept, keeping concept growth narrow and evidence-backed.
4. It improves the treatment of HC-09 and HC-11 without opening a broad new governance family.
5. It is more readable as a practitioner-facing diagnostic than Experiment A.
6. It is more operationally efficient as a default than Experiment C because it reaches the same stabilized endpoint without relying on exploratory inflation as part of the working story.

## Narrow hybrid note

No broad hybrid rewrite is warranted.

If a narrow hybrid rule is desired for downstream control, define it as follows:

- Use **Experiment B's final structure** as the working default.
- Retain **Experiment A's presumption against new concept growth** as the first decision rule.
- Retain **Experiment C's stress-test finding** only as a boundary proof that looser exploration still collapses back to one bounded overlay after pruning.
- Keep `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship` explicitly limited to **HC-09** and **HC-11** unless future evidence clears the same admission-and-pruning threshold.

This is a governance rule for use, not a rewrite of the taxonomy.

## Recommended next orchestration step

Carry **Experiment B** forward as the working default into the next v4 drafting step, with one explicit implementation guardrail: preserve the six-pillar kernel as the main structure and treat `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship` only as a thin, evidence-bounded overlay for HC-09 and HC-11. Retain **Experiment A** as the conservative no-growth benchmark and **Experiment C** as the boundary-validation record.
