## Interpretation

Experiment C concluded that most apparent pressure for concept growth still comes from cross-pillar activation burdens rather than from a truly missing governance family. Compared with Experiments A and B, the exploratory settings made it easier to provisionally admit two community-mediated concepts in Round 2 for the two softest cases:
- HC-09 food-system and productive bio-resource stock
- HC-11 human and social capability stock

However, Round 3 applied the approved very high pruning pressure and removed the inflated two-concept outcome. The final result converged with Experiment B's stabilized shape: one narrow overlay for informal stewardship, customary knowledge, trust-based self-organization, and autonomous collective action that remain weakly legible inside the six formal readiness categories alone.

This is the main value of Experiment C. It probed the outer boundary of concept need and showed that, even when exploratory admission is allowed, the evidence does not support more than one bounded overlay after aggressive pruning. Relative to Experiment A, Experiment C is less conservative because it allows and tests provisional growth before pruning. Relative to Experiment B, it is more exploratory in Round 2 but ends in the same stabilized concept count after Round 3.
