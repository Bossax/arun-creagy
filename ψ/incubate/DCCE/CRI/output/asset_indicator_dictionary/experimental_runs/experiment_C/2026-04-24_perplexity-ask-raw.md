# Experiment C Raw Perplexity Ask Capture

## Exact prompt packet

System:
`You are assisting a controlled urban resilience taxonomy experiment. Use only web-grounded evidence with citations. Be concise and analytical.`

User:
`For an urban resilience governance taxonomy, assess whether the activation gaps for (1) food-system and productive bio-resource stock and (2) human/social capability stock are best represented as: Option A) fully foldable into existing formal readiness categories; Option B) two separate new concepts; or Option C) one merged thin overlay centered on community self-organization, stewardship, and traditional/local knowledge. Return a compact markdown table with columns: option, evidence-grounded rationale, strengths, weaknesses, and which asset families it fits best. Then give a short conclusion stating which option is most defensible if pruning aggressiveness is very high and concept inflation must be avoided.`

## Raw answer (verbatim)

# Assessment of Activation Gaps in Urban Resilience Governance Taxonomy

| Option | Evidence-Grounded Rationale | Strengths | Weaknesses | Asset Families Fit |
|--------|---------------------------|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| **A: Fold into Existing Categories** | Current governance frameworks emphasize "systems approach" and "multi-scale governance" as overarching principles[4]. Food systems and human capability could theoretically map to existing resilience dimensions (structural, social, economic)[4]. | Minimizes conceptual redundancy; maintains parsimony; leverages established categorical infrastructure. | Food-system stocks exhibit distinct structural dependencies and supply-chain fragility[1] not adequately captured by generic readiness categories. Social capability requires explicit attention to co-production, trust-building, and knowledge integration[4]—currently marginalized in formal governance structures[1]. Risks obscuring activation barriers specific to productive assets and community agency. | Generic cross-cutting domains; misses sector-specific vulnerabilities. |
| **B: Two Separate New Concepts** | Systematic review identifies four distinct vulnerability dimensions including "structural dependency on external supplies" and governance fragmentation[1]. Food system vulnerabilities concentrate in supply-chain complexity, local production capacity, and equity access[1]. Social capability gaps stem from "departmental fragmentation, unclear responsibilities, and poor coordination"[1] requiring explicit "knowledge co-production and trust"[4]. | Precise differentiation enables targeted intervention design. Food-system activation addresses storage, distribution diversity, and local production readiness[1]. Social capability activation targets cross-sectoral coordination skills and community engagement mechanisms[4]. Aligns with evidence on governance coordination failures[1]. | Doubles concept count, increasing taxonomy complexity and potential for inconsistent application. Creates artificial separation between interdependent stocks (e.g., urban agriculture requires both physical infrastructure and community stewardship capacity). | Food production and supply-chain infrastructure; governance coordination and organizational capacity. |
| **C: Merged Overlay on Community Self-Organization** | Urban food systems require "pluralistic governance systems that engage market entities, social organizations, and community networks"[1]. Community food networks "integrate consumption resilience with social cohesion and governance capacity-building"[1]. Informal systems provide "critical flexibility during disruptions"[1]. | Economical conceptually; captures emergent adaptive capacity. Emphasizes practical community agency over top-down readiness metrics. Reflects how traditional/local knowledge and stewardship underpin both food access equity and supply-chain flexibility[1]. | Risks diluting specificity of material (food stock) constraints. Community self-organization alone cannot substitute for infrastructure redundancy or supply-chain diversity[1]. May obscure need for formal governance coordination[1] and institutional resource allocation. Informal systems complement but cannot replace formal planning for systemic resilience[1]. | Community networks; informal supply chains; local knowledge systems; equity-focused interventions. |

## Conclusion

**Option B (two separate new concepts) is most defensible under aggressive pruning constraints.** While it increases total concept count by two, each addresses a distinct activation barrier with unique intervention levers: food-system stocks require infrastructure and supply-chain redundancy measures[1], while human/social capability stocks require governance coordination mechanisms and co-production frameworks[4]. Option A obscures these specifics within generic categories; Option C conflates material constraints with governance capacity, risking false equivalence between community stewardship and physical infrastructure readiness. The evidence shows these gaps persist precisely *because* they remain marginalized in existing frameworks[1][2]. Two discrete, precisely-defined concepts enable targeted diagnosis and measurement—critical for implementation under resource constraints.
