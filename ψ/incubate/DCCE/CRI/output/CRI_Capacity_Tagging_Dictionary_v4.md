# CRI Capacity Tagging Dictionary v4: Adopted Working Version

## 1. Methodological conclusion: preserve the kernel and adopt a complementary-signal reading

This version of the dictionary records the adopted working position after the Phase 2 v4 experiment review. **Governance** remains organized as an **Institutional Readiness** framework grounded in the same six operational pillars carried forward from v3.

The adopted v4 move is narrower than a redesign. It keeps the six-pillar kernel, accepts **Experiment B** as the working default, and treats asset indicators and governance indicators as **complementary signals of resilience conditions**.

This dictionary does **not** use asset-governance linkages as public calculation groups. If the expression `R = A × G` is used in discussion, it should be read only as a conceptual reminder that both asset conditions and governance conditions matter. It is **not** the operational scoring rule in this dictionary.

### Why keep the "Institutional Readiness" lens?
We continue to prefer a readiness-based functional framework because:
1. **Actionability:** it identifies concrete institutional jobs that Mayors, Governors, and line agencies actually manage.
2. **Data alignment:** it remains legible to LPA-style and Thai administrative audit surfaces that are structured as readiness checks.
3. **SETS consistency:** it preserves Governance as the **Institutional domain** that activates Social, Ecological, and Technological stock into realized service function.
4. **Comparability:** it allows Experiment A, B, and C to test linkage logic on a common benchmark rather than re-opening the kernel itself.

### What changes in v4?
- The **six-pillar kernel is not changed**.
- The existing v3 concept inventory remains the main starting surface for indicator screening.
- **Experiment B** is adopted as the working default because it preserves the kernel while keeping concept growth narrow and evidence-bounded.
- Asset indicators and governance indicators are read **together**, but not as direct formula inputs and not as linkage-based public calculation groups.
- Activation chains remain available for **internal interpretation only**. They help explain how stock may or may not become usable function, but they are not the public calculation engine.
- `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship` is integrated as a **scored cross-cutting governance concept** that changes which metrics are admitted into relevant pillar scores.
- Experiment A remains the conservative no-growth benchmark and Experiment C remains the stress-test boundary record.
- Stronger asset and governance scores may suggest stronger resilience conditions in general, but they do not by themselves prove realized resilience. Empirical monitoring is still required.

## 2. Frozen structure: the 6 pillars of institutional readiness

For the `governance_pillar` column, we retain these 6 canonical functions:

1. **Planning & Strategy (The Blueprint):** Integration of resilience into mandates and long-term visions.
2. **Finance & Procurement (The Fuel):** The flow and velocity of budget for resilience and emergency response.
3. **Coordination & Partnerships (The Connective Tissue):** Multi-stakeholder collaboration and transboundary (cross-scale) management.
4. **Service Delivery & Ops (The Interface):** The front-line performance and responsiveness of climate-relevant services.
5. **Data & Digital Governance (The Intelligence):** The systems that enable risk-informed decisions and community agency.
6. **HR & Institutional Capacity (The Muscle):** The technical skills, training, and headcount required to execute.

## 3. Internal interpretation layer and boundary rules

This layer is intentionally lightweight and mainly **internal-facing**. It helps the team interpret how asset stock may or may not become usable service function, without turning that internal reasoning into a public scoring engine.

### 3.1 Boundary rule 1: complementary signals, not direct formula inputs
- Asset indicators and governance indicators are read together as complementary signals of resilience conditions.
- They are **not** direct formula inputs in this dictionary.
- They are **not** grouped into public linkage-based calculation blocks.
- A concept may still influence the score-producing metric set by shaping which indicators are admitted into pillar scoring.

### 3.2 Boundary rule 2: stock-first, activation-aware reading
- Asset stock is treated as **structural potential**, not realized resilience.
- Governance conditions help explain whether that potential is more likely to become usable function.
- This reading supports interpretation. It does not, on its own, prove realized resilience.

### 3.3 Boundary rule 3: activation chains are internal interpretation tools
- Activation chains are kept for internal interpretation, proxy screening, and explanation.
- They help the team surface plausible activation logic and explain one main bottleneck at a time.
- They are **not** the public calculation engine.
- They should appear in external communication only as simple example narratives, not as scoring mechanics.

### 3.4 Boundary rule 4: one primary bottleneck per internal chain
- Each internal chain should identify one **primary bottleneck** that most clearly explains where stock may fail to become function.
- Secondary frictions may be noted internally, but the chain should remain readable and decision-oriented.

### 3.5 Boundary rule 5: reuse-first decision ladder
Every unresolved linkage must be tested in this order:
1. Reuse existing v3 concept as-is.
2. Reuse existing v3 concept with an interpretation note.
3. Combine two or more existing v3 concepts into one activation chain.
4. Add a new concept provisionally.
5. Keep the new concept only if it survives evidence and pruning review.

### 3.6 Boundary rule 6: cross-cutting concepts can shape scoring without changing the six-pillar kernel
- The six pillars remain the general operating system.
- Cross-cutting governance concepts may be integrated when they improve what metrics are scanned, admitted, and scored across multiple pillars.
- In the adopted working version, `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship` is retained as a scored cross-cutting concept. Metrics captured through this lens must be assigned back into relevant pillar scores rather than left as narrative only.
- This integration does not create a seventh governance pillar, a separate public score, or a visible activation-chain mechanic.

### 3.7 Boundary rule 7: evidence discipline and validation
- External-first evidence remains the required standard for any new concept or reinterpretation that survives beyond a draft hypothesis.
- Every reuse, combination, or addition decision should be logged in the experiment artifacts before it is stabilized.
- Higher asset and governance scores may suggest stronger resilience conditions in general, but they do not by themselves prove realized resilience.
- The validity of any activation-chain reading is context dependent and should be checked through empirical monitoring against observed outcomes.

### 3.8 Internal chain notation
For internal v4 interpretation work, each chain should stay readable in the following compact form:
1. **Asset stock** — what enabling structure exists.
2. **Selected governance block(s)** — which pillar job(s) activate it.
3. **Service function** — what the stock should deliver under stress.
4. **Primary bottleneck** — where activation most clearly fails.

This notation is for internal interpretation, not for public scoring mechanics.

## 5. Concept definitions (adopted working reference for indicator scanning)

This section keeps the v3 kernel definitions as the main reference surface, but they should now be read through the adopted v4 framing.

For screening candidate indicators:
- read each concept as a **condition signal**, not as a formula term;
- prefer metrics that show whether the function is usable, continuous, and accessible under stress;
- if a metric is selected through a cross-cutting concept, score it inside its assigned pillar rather than as a separate public score;
- use internal interpretation notes only to sharpen proxy reading, not to create public scoring steps.

Items marked `*(design placeholder)*` remain provisional candidate concepts. They are not evidence that Experiment B added new adopted governance pillars.

### 5.1 Planning & Strategy

- **Plan revision cycle** — The frequency and quality of updating core plans (climate/DRM, land use, development) in light of new risk information, demographic change, and learning from past events. A high-capacity system institutionalises iterative review, monitoring, and post-event learning rather than treating plans as static documents.
- **Risk-informed planning** — The degree to which multi-hazard risk assessments and vulnerability diagnostics are embedded in land-use, infrastructure, and investment planning. This includes using hazard maps and multi-risk diagnostics to steer siting, zoning, and capital budgeting.
- **Policy Integration** — The strength of cross-sectoral and multi-level coordination mechanisms that align climate resilience objectives across departments and tiers of government. Integration is reflected in joint strategies, shared data platforms, and participatory processes that break sectoral silos.

### 5.2 Finance & Procurement

- **Emergency budget flow** — The speed, flexibility, and reliability with which funds can be mobilised and disbursed during and after shocks to protect basic services and support recovery. This includes contingency reserves, disaster funds, flexible budget lines, and risk-transfer instruments.
- **Climate budget tagging** — The existence and use of budget classifications or tagging systems that identify climate-relevant spending (adaptation and mitigation). This enables tracking, reporting, and reallocating public finance toward climate-resilient investments over time.
- 🫥**Asset Mobilization Velocity** ~~*(design placeholder)*~~ — Conceptually, the speed at which public, private, and community assets (equipment, facilities, supplies) can be mobilised and deployed to front-line response. Formal definitions and measurement patterns are **pending additional evidence** (for example Consensus.ai or sector-specific studies).
- **Resource allocation scale** — The magnitude and coverage of financial resources dedicated to resilience and emergency functions relative to the overall budget and risk profile. It captures both the existence of dedicated reserves and the share of spending plausibly related to resilience and disaster management.

### 5.3 Coordination & Partnerships

- **Multi-stakeholder collab.** — The density and quality of structured interactions between government, private sector, academia, civil society, and vulnerable communities (for example Shared Learning Dialogues). High capacity is reflected in institutionalised mechanisms that surface diverse knowledge and jointly steer resilience priorities.
- 🫥**Distributed Command Agility** ~~*(design placeholder)*~~ — Conceptually, the ability of leadership and command functions to decentralise, delegate, and re-route decision-making authority during crises (for example SOPs for local delegation). Specific empirical measures are **pending** until supporting literature is added.
- 🫥**Transboundary Feedbacks** ~~*(design placeholder)*~~ — Conceptually, the ability of governance arrangements to recognise and manage cross-jurisdictional and cross-scale feedbacks (for example upstream-downstream watershed effects). Concrete definitions and metrics are **pending** additional sources.
- **Formal coordination bodies** — The existence and mandate of formal cross-departmental working groups, taskforces, or coordination bodies (for example inter-office climate risk groups, resilience committees) that steer resilience strategy and link fragmented departments.

### 5.4 Service Delivery & Ops

- **Service delivery timeliness** — The rapidity with which essential climate-relevant services (emergency response, utilities, social protection) can respond to and recover from disruptions. It covers both response time (time to first action) and recovery time (time to restore acceptable service levels).
- 🫥 **Case throughput rate** *(partially evidenced)* — The rate at which complaints, incidents, or service requests (for example Traffy Fondue cases) are processed and resolved. While throughput is discussed in transport and service literature, the exact label and operationalisation in this corpus are limited; this concept should be used cautiously and refined as new sources are added. 
- 🫥 **Digital Social Mediation** ~~*(design placeholder)*~~ — Conceptually, the use of digital platforms, social media, and peer-to-peer tools to match needs and resources (for example crowdsourced aid-matching). Formal definitions and metrics (reach, latency, inclusiveness) are **pending** explicit literature.
- **Infrastructure robustness** — The capacity of physical assets and networks (transport, energy, water, social infrastructure) to maintain function or fail safely under shocks. It is typically assessed through structural design standards, performance under stress, and the presence of protective and redundancy measures. %% this sounds like an asset indicator %%

### 5.5 Data & Digital Governance

- **Data interoperability** — The degree to which data can be shared, integrated, and used across departments and sectors through interoperable systems, shared standards, and formal data-sharing agreements. High capacity implies whole-of-government platforms and cross-sector digital infrastructures that support shared situational awareness.
- **Digital climate services** — The deployment of digital tools (early warning systems, digital twins, real-time dashboards, geo-tagged data platforms) to monitor, predict, and communicate climate-related risks. It reflects the maturity of data pipelines, analytical capabilities, and citizen-facing channels.
- **Risk Assessment depth** — The breadth and sophistication of risk diagnostics, moving from single-hazard, technical studies toward multi-risk, participatory assessments that incorporate cascading impacts, systemic fragilities, and institutional constraints.

### 5.6 HR & Institutional Capacity

- **Emergency staff capacity** — The availability, distribution, and competencies of personnel required to manage crises (emergency response teams, technical staff, specialised health personnel). It captures both headcount and institutional arrangements (dedicated teams, cross-functional groups).
- **Staff Training frequency** *(partially evidenced)* — The regularity and coverage of training programmes related to resilience, risk management, and emergency operations. Current evidence is indirect (for example after-action learning and capability indices); explicit training-frequency metrics should be locked in only when clear source support is available.

### 5.7 Cross-cutting scored concept (used through pillar-assigned metrics)

- **Community Self-Organization & Stewardship** *(cross-cutting scored concept)* — The degree to which communities can organize, maintain collective routines, and co-produce risk-relevant services with public institutions before, during, and after shocks. In this dictionary, the concept changes which candidate metrics are considered valid for pillar scoring where formal institutional indicators alone would miss local collective capacity. It is therefore operational in the scoring workflow, but only through metrics assigned back into existing pillars. It is **not** a seventh pillar, **not** a separate public score, and **not** a public activation-chain display.

## 6. Adoption boundary, lineage, and validation

### 6.1 Adopted working default

- The adopted working default is **Experiment B**.
- The six-pillar institutional-readiness kernel remains the main governance structure.
- Asset indicators and governance indicators should be communicated as complementary signals of resilience conditions.
- Stakeholder-facing outputs should show asset scores and six governance pillar scores; any `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship` influence remains embedded in the metric set rather than shown as a separate chain or score.

### 6.2 Cross-cutting scored integration rule

- `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship` is integrated as a cross-cutting scored concept for metric scanning and pillar score construction.
- Metrics selected under this concept must be assigned back into relevant pillar scores rather than left as narrative only.
- It is not a seventh pillar, not a separate public score, and not a visible activation-chain mechanic.

### 6.3 Lineage record

- **Experiment A** is preserved as the conservative no-growth benchmark.
- **Experiment C** is preserved as the stress-test boundary record.
- Both remain part of the design history, but neither replaces the adopted Experiment B logic.

### 6.4 Validation caveat

- Higher asset and governance scores may suggest stronger resilience conditions in general.
- They do not by themselves prove realized resilience.
- Activation-chain validity remains context dependent and should be checked through later monitoring against observed outcomes.
