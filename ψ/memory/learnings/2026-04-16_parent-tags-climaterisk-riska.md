# Synthesis: Comprehensive Risk Management (CRM) - Full Structure

This note synthesizes the current status of knowledge of comprehensive risk management. After a few months of grappling with a multitude of frameworks and technical terms, I have come to a sound understanding of the theoretical framework of comprehensive risk management (CRM). In this context, CRM is a comprehensive framework which consists of inputs, outputs, processes, and relationships among them which dictate the flow of information. CRM encompasses often-deal-separately fields including risk assessment, disaster risk reduction, climate mitigation, climate adaptation, decision-making, financing, and evaluation into a homogenous narrative.

## Core Concepts & Definition of Risk
- **Risk**: Potential for adverse consequences for human and ecological systems. It is *value-based* and context-specific.
- **Shift in Perspective**: To align DRR and CCA, the definition of risk in the DRM community needs to shift from hazard-focused to impact-focused.
- **Uncertainty**: grows with time. 
    - **Short-term**: Analytical/quantitative approaches (probabilistic modeling, DRM) are suitable.
    - **Medium/Long-term**: Semi-quantitative (indicator/index-based) or qualitative (expert elicitation) approaches are required due to unquantifiable uncertainties (emissions, exposure, vulnerability, cascading impacts).

## Risk Assessment Approaches
1. **Analytical/Quantitative**: Probabilistic catastrophe models (DRM standard).
2. **Semi-Quantitative**: Indicator/index-based, using impact chains or expert judgment.
3. **Qualitative**: Expert elicitation and systemic reviews to fill data gaps.

## Comprehensive Risk Assessment & Impact Chains
- Stresses compounding risks from climate and non-climate drivers.
- **Impact Chain Analysis**: A participatory technique for conceptualizing risk by mapping cause-effect relationships of hazards, exposure, and vulnerability.
- **Nature Risk**: Overlaps with climate risk within this framework.

## Risk Assessment Workflow
1. **Scoping**: Context, goals, boundaries, risk criteria.
2. **Risk Identification**: Past data, climate impact studies, systems of interest.
3. **Risk Analysis**: Impact chains, vulnerability, interrelations, synthesis.
4. **Risk Evaluation**: Compare results against risk criteria (acceptable, tolerable, intolerable).

## Decision-Making Space & Risk Layering
- **Risk Layering** (Mechler, 2014): Measures and financing allocated based on frequency and magnitude.
    - **Low-risk (High freq, Low impact)**: Risk reduction (DRR/CCA) is most cost-efficient.
    - **Medium-risk**: Residual risk transferred via risk financing (insurance).
    - **High-risk (Low freq, High impact)**: Absorbed by regional/international aid (Loss & Damage).

## Measures Portfolio
- **Structural/Hard**: Infrastructure solutions.
- **Non-structural/Soft**: Capacity building, laws, early warning.
- **Nature-based Solutions (NbS)**: Ecosystem-based adaptation (no/low-regret).
- **Social Protection/Financial**: Cash transfers, insurance.
- **Transformational**: Fundamental system changes (resettlement, livelihood diversification).

## Financing Spectrum
- **Finance for Risk Management**: DRR (short feedback loop) and CCA (long-term transformative).
- **Climate Finance**: Traditionally focused on mitigation.
- **Risk Financing**: Tackles residual risk (insurance).
- **Loss and Damage Financing**: Tackles risk above hard limits.

## Key Conclusions
- DRR and CCA serve the same goal but target different time frames within the risk layering spectrum.
- Nature finance overlaps with adaptation, mitigation, and DRR.
- Decision-making should be inclusive, iterative (adaptive management), and based on societal risk tolerance.
