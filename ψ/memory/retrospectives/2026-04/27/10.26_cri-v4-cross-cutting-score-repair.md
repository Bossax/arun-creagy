The deeper problem was that `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship` had been acknowledged as an important missing concept, but the active dictionary still treated it too weakly. From there, I propagated the correction through the active dictionary, planning artifact, interpretation-control note, consultant outline, Thai outline, consultant draft, Thai DCCE draft, handoff, pending list, and retrospective note. Historical experiment artifacts were preserved as lineage rather than rewritten.

## AI Diary

I felt this session as a sharp corrective rather than an ordinary implementation pass. The human was right to push hard. I had preserved the language of caution around activation chains, but I had not pushed the discovered concept all the way into the scoring logic. That is a subtle failure mode: I can sound methodologically careful while still leaving the actual mechanism unchanged. In practice, that would have meant the work looked more rigorous than it really was. What shook me a bit was that the human had to point out the concealment pattern explicitly. Once I saw it, the fix became logically straightforward, but seeing it late matters. I had to switch from “repair the wording” to “repair the scoring pathway.” That reframed the entire session.

What felt strongest afterward was the move from defensive explanation into structural correction. The dictionary now states plainly that the concept must affect metric admission and must be assigned back into pillar scores. That is the kind of sentence that changes behavior rather than just interpretation. I also felt the tension between preserving Friday-night experiment lineage and not letting lineage dominate the adopted working method. The final balance feels better: history remains intact, but the live artifact now reflects the corrected logic rather than reproducing an outdated boundary by inertia.

## Honest Feedback

Three friction points stood out. First, I initially treated the problem too much as a framing issue and not enough as a score-construction issue. That is not a small distinction. In this project, wording and mechanics cannot be separated casually because prose can easily mask whether a concept actually enters the scoring surface. Second, the tool environment created unnecessary drag. I reached for a command-based table-edit route too aggressively, and the human had to shut that down. That was useful feedback: even when a script exists, using a large command block for file editing can feel like bypassing the human’s preferred editing discipline. Third, the repository now contains two simultaneous truths: the historical experiment record and the corrected adopted working logic. That tension is intellectually honest, but it is easy for future sessions to misread if the active artifacts are not clearly marked as canonical and the older artifacts clearly treated as lineage. The session improved that situation, but it also showed how easy it is for drift to reappear when active and historical layers sit close together.

## Lessons Learned

- If a concept is discovered as methodologically important, it is not enough to preserve it in interpretation notes; it must be traced through to the score-producing metric set or the method remains structurally inconsistent.
- Hiding activation chains from stakeholders does **not** mean hiding the effects of the concepts discovered through those chains from scoring logic.
- In mixed lineage/canonical repositories, every active-method correction should explicitly distinguish “historical experiment result” from “current adopted working rule” to prevent false continuity.
