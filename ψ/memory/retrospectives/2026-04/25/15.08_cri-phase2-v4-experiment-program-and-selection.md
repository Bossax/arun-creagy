## Session Summary
This session completed the full CRI Phase 2 v4 experiment program. The work moved from execution-plan approval into structured implementation: building the v4 scaffold, initializing all experimental artifacts, running Experiments A, B, and C across the 11 hardened asset concepts, and synthesizing the comparison into a final selection memo. The final outcome selected Experiment B as the preferred working default because it preserved the six-pillar kernel, achieved full coverage across all 11 asset concepts, and introduced only one tightly bounded overlay for the two hardest outlier cases rather than expanding the taxonomy broadly.

## Architecture Decisions
- The six-pillar institutional-readiness kernel remains the primary governance structure.
- Asset stocks are treated as potential that require activation through governance processes.
- No broad seventh governance family is justified by current evidence.
- The Friday-night experiment result first stabilized `Community Self-Organization & Stewardship` as a bounded overlay for HC-09 and HC-11; the later working-default correction then carried that pressure forward as a **cross-cutting scored concept** for metric selection and pillar scoring without creating a seventh pillar or a public activation-chain display.
- Experiment A is retained as a conservative benchmark and Experiment C as boundary-validation evidence, but neither replaces the Experiment B working default.

## AI Diary

The temptation throughout was to let the framework become more elegant by growing it, because growth often feels like intellectual progress. But the real test here was not whether I could generate more categories. It was whether I could hold the structure still, stress it from several angles, and discover whether it truly needed expansion. That made the work slower but more honest. I had to keep resisting the tendency to treat a vague discomfort as proof that a new concept was required. Instead, I had to follow the chain from asset stock, to expected function, to governance activation, to bottleneck, and ask whether the discomfort was merely an interpretive weakness or a genuine ontological gap.

I also noticed that this session demanded a different kind of patience than ordinary drafting. The human did not want a quick answer or a polished single-pass method. They wanted a design that could survive inspection. That meant the process itself had to become an artifact: experiments, rounds, pruning logs, comparison memo, handoff. By the end, I felt more confident not because the result was complex, but because the comparison kept collapsing toward the same narrow conclusion. That recurrence gave the final selection real weight. The most satisfying part was that the outcome preserved methodological discipline: the kernel remained intact, and the one surviving overlay stayed bounded rather than metastasizing into a new taxonomy.

## Honest Feedback
Three friction points stood out. First, there was friction between the desired methodological rigor and the mechanics of execution. The human wanted a disciplined experimental program, but the tool environment occasionally pushed toward improvised workarounds. That mismatch made the workflow noisier than it should have been. Second, the repository now contains a substantial amount of experiment trace material, which is good for auditability but increases cognitive load. Without a deliberate cleanup or promotion pass, the final adopted method risks being buried under its own history. Third, I can see how easy it would be for a future session to overread the surviving overlay concept and let it creep into a broader governance family. The memo does state the boundary clearly, but boundaries weaken over time unless they are encoded into the canonical artifact itself. The next session should therefore focus less on more experimentation and more on tightening the adoption boundary so the result cannot drift.

## Lessons Learned
- Comparative method design is stronger than one-pass method drafting when the real question is whether structure needs to grow.
- Full coverage across all 11 concepts matters more than a convenient pilot if the output is meant to justify the framework itself.
- A bounded overlay can be acceptable without undermining the kernel, but the adoption step must still check whether the discovered pressure point is actually entering the score-producing metric set.
