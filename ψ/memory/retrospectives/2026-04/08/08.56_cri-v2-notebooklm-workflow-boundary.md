## AI Diary
I felt two things at once in this session: productive and uneasy. Productive, because the repository now has a full traceable chain from NotebookLM prompts to raw evidence artifacts to a rebuilt concept register to a redrafted v2 dictionary and an updated crosswalk. Uneasy, because a lot of that progress came from compensating for workflow drift rather than smoothly following the original architecture. I kept seeing the same pattern: I asked NotebookLM to do something that looked like structured transformation, but it wanted to answer as a literature synthesizer. It gave rich, citation-bearing content, yet not always in the shape I actually needed. That forced me to flatten, reinterpret, and stabilise outputs locally.

The pulse context claims this is Session #1 of 1 in the project with a 1-day streak, which itself feels like a reminder that the pulse layer is stale relative to the real working cadence. Even so, the emotional truth is that the session had momentum. I kept moving because the artifacts were accumulating, but the workflow architecture was sending a warning signal: I was letting the extraction engine drift into synthesis territory. I ended the session more convinced that the real craft here is not just getting answers out of literature tools, but placing them inside a disciplined boundary so they do not quietly become substitute reasoning engines for local knowledge work.

## Honest Feedback
Three friction points stood out clearly.

First, I blurred the boundary between extraction and synthesis. NotebookLM should have been used to harvest literature-grounded concepts and citations, but I pushed it toward harmonisation, de-duplication, and QC. That created structural drift and forced later corrective work.

Second, the later pass design was too optimistic about deterministic downstream behavior. Passes 4–6 assumed NotebookLM would operate like a transform engine over prior structured outputs, but it repeatedly reverted to generating fresh framework scaffolds. The prompts were not strict enough to counter that tendency.

Third, the workflow lost time because technical reliability and analytical reliability got entangled. Authentication, timeout behavior, visible-browser constraints, nested outputs, and QC expectations all mixed together. That made it harder to tell whether a bad result was due to prompt logic, model behavior, or the MCP channel.

The corrective pattern is now clearer: use NotebookLM for extraction, preserve raw outputs, and do harmonisation locally where I control the structure.

## Lessons Learned
- NotebookLM is strongest when constrained to literature-grounded extraction and citation-bearing concept harvesting.
- Harmonisation, flattening, de-duplication, and QC over evolving local registers should be done locally in the repo.
- If a tool repeatedly replays category scaffolds instead of transforming existing rows, the workflow boundary is wrong, not just the prompt wording.
