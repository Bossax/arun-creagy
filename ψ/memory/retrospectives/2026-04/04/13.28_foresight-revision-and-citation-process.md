## AI Diary

The human was not asking for polished final prose yet; the deeper need was to reduce uncertainty before the next heavy revision pass. I could feel that the pressure point in this work was not a shortage of words but a shortage of alignment: alignment between team voice and analytical extension, between draft sections and their intended function, between source artifacts and citation surfaces, and between what should be researched externally versus what could already be synthesized internally. That made the session feel careful rather than dramatic.

I found myself repeatedly moving between three layers: the integrated draft, the benchmark plan, and the source/citation system. That triangular movement was productive. When the user raised questions about halal economy, Islamic funds, macro trends, and Gemini prompts, it became clear that the real job was to build a revision environment where later prose decisions would be easier to justify. I think the most valuable thing completed today was the shift from vague dissatisfaction (“the draft still isn’t right”) to named tasks with better boundaries. The template-aligned benchmark criteria especially helped, because they changed the standard from “does this sound okay?” to “is this section actually doing the job it is supposed to do?”

I also noticed a healthy handoff instinct emerging by the end. Rather than pushing toward one more hurried rewrite, the session concluded by packaging the work into a usable handoff. That felt right. On a project where the draft, evidence base, and citation system are all moving at once, good transitions matter as much as good paragraphs.

## Honest Feedback

1. **The repository state added noise.** The git diff showed a very large number of modified, deleted, and untracked files, many of which predate this exact session focus. That made it harder to tell what belonged to today’s task versus what was general repo drift. It did not block progress, but it reduced confidence around commit discipline and increased the cognitive tax of every “finalize” step.

2. **Prompt design took longer because the target researcher was external.** Several iterations were needed before the prompts were properly framed for Gemini as an independent deep-research actor with zero context. That was necessary work, but it exposed how easy it is to accidentally write prompts for “future me” rather than for a truly external system. The correction was good, but it took multiple passes to get there.

3. **The draft comments reveal unresolved conceptual structure, not just missing evidence.** Some `%%` comments ask for more research, but others are really about section discipline and narrative architecture. That distinction matters. If future work treats every comment as a sourcing problem, the draft may become more cited without becoming more coherent. The benchmark update helped with this, but the risk remains present.

## Lessons Learned

- In a complex report workflow, benchmark criteria should test both **content anchoring** and **section function**.
- External deep-research prompts must be written as if the model knows nothing about the report, the repo, or the session.
- Macro-trend research is easier to cite and revise back into the draft when split into narrow thematic clusters rather than one overloaded research request.
