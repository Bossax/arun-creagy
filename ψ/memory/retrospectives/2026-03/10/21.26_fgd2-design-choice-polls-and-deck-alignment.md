## AI Diary (first-person)

I felt the center of gravity shift from “writing documents” to “designing a room.” The moment the slide deck text appeared in the inbox, it became obvious how easily language can drift: a deck needs confidence and clarity, but a strategy artifact needs precision and scope-control. MVP-2 was the clearest example—calling it a “routine pipeline” sounds reassuring to an audience, but it quietly commits us to automation and operational load that nobody has actually agreed to yet.

The most satisfying part was rewriting the polls. Early questions were polite, but they collected sentiment and didn’t reduce ambiguity. Once I reframed them as forced choices (default entry principle, pick-2 MVP commitments, link-first vs host-first, who signs off), the questions started producing outputs that can become a decision log. I also noticed the emotional undercurrent: accountability fear is not a technical issue; it’s a publishing-operations issue. Designing governance questions that probe practicality (who can review, what minimum gate is realistic) felt like respecting the lived constraints of the team.

## Honest Feedback (friction points)

1) The repo working tree has a lot of pre-existing modified/untracked state, which makes it hard to cleanly attribute what changed “today” versus older ongoing edits. That increases the chance of accidental drift between the deck narrative, the canonical artifacts, and what actually gets executed.

2) Workshop language is a double-edged sword: it must be simple, but when it is too decisive (e.g., “routine pipeline”) it becomes a hidden commitment. This is especially risky for MVP-2 and for any language that implies “single source of truth” without naming the endorsement authority and audit trail.

3) Poll design needed multiple passes. The first drafts were too general and would have produced agreement without actionable next steps. It took deliberate iteration to ensure each poll answer maps to a specific design choice that can be written into the artifacts after the meeting.

## Lessons Learned

- Polls must be designed so each answer maps to a concrete decision (navigation stance, MVP commitments, boundary stance, minimum publishing gates, endorsement authority), otherwise the workshop produces discussion but not closure.
- Scope realism should be explicitly polled (especially MVP-2), otherwise the room infers automation timelines that were never agreed.

## Next Steps

- Use the sitemap-only and governance-only poll sets as the default Mentimeter block; add only 1–2 additional decision-bank questions if time allows.
- After FGD2, convert poll outcomes into a decision log update and tighten wording across MVP v3 and governance v3 where necessary.
