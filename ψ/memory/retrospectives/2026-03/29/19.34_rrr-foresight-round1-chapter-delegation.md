## Architecture Decisions
- **Chapter-separated drafting is the unit of work**: Round 1 outputs must remain separate per chapter, with explicit boundaries for what is allowed to be drafted.
- **Evidence trace is mandatory**: each chapter draft must be paired with an evidence-trace/audit note.
- **Citation chain must terminate in real sources**: draft prose cannot cite internal markdown artifacts as if they were sources; internal artifacts may be used only as analysis intermediates.
- **Actors anchored to field knowledge**: actor filling begins with Duly’s field-informed note, then cross-checked with other artifacts.

## AI Diary (first-person)
I felt the session shift from “research execution” into “writing reliability engineering.” The core tension was that we had new research artifacts, but a report can still fail if citations are not auditable or if the draft collapses into one blended document that hides uncertainty. I focused on establishing constraints that force honesty: chapter-separated outputs, and explicit HOLD/GAP markers wherever we cannot trace a claim back to a real source. I also noticed how quickly internal markdown artifacts can become accidental “sources” unless the workflow makes that impossible. The chapter-by-chapter delegation helped reduce cognitive load; it turned a sprawling workspace into three bounded problems with their own outputs and audit ledgers. Anchoring actors to the Duly field note felt especially important, because it prevents the actor map from becoming generic and detached from lived reality. At the same time, I’m aware the pulse metadata looked stale, which is a reminder that operational telemetry can drift from actual work; I treated it as optional context rather than ground truth.

## Honest Feedback
1) **Automation fragility**: the Deep Research automation path showed brittleness (subscriber interruptions, missing responses). Even if research is done manually, the workflow needs a robust “capture and distill” step so results don’t disappear into UI-only outputs.
2) **Citation trace overhead**: enforcing real-source citations dramatically increases the workload. Without a shared, canonical “source ledger” that records the final citeable sources, drafting will stall and HOLD/GAP sections will proliferate.
3) **Cross-mode friction**: jumping between planning, coding, and orchestration can cause confusion about what is being edited and what is being executed. The chapter package pattern helps, but it still needs a single top-level index that declares what is draft-ready now.

## Lessons Learned
- A chapter draft without an evidence-trace ledger is not a draft; it is a risk surface.
- The single easiest way to reduce hallucinated authority is to ban markdown-to-markdown citations in sponsor-facing prose.
- Actor maps need a grounded anchor (field note / interview) to avoid generic stakeholder lists.
